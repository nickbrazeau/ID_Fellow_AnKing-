## Question

What determines **cross-reactivity** between penicillins and cephalosporins, and which cephalosporins share R1 side chains with amoxicillin?

## Answer

### **Key Principle:**
**R1 side chain** determines cross-reactivity (NOT the β-lactam ring itself)

### **Why R1 > R2 Side Chain:**
- β-lactam ring breaks → R2 side chain is **lost** during degradation
- R1 side chain **remains intact** → causes IgE recognition
- Cross-reactivity occurs when penicillin and cephalosporin have **identical R1 side chains**

### **Cross-Reactivity Rates:**

| R1 Side Chain | Cross-Reactivity Risk |
|---------------|----------------------|
| **Identical R1** (e.g., amoxicillin → cephalexin) | **14-40%** |
| **Different R1** | <3% (same as general population) |
| **No shared side chains** | <1% |

### **Amoxicillin/Ampicillin R1 Side Chain Matches:**

**HIGH RISK - Avoid these if amoxicillin anaphylaxis:**
- Cephalexin (Keflex)
- Cefadroxil
- Cefprozil
- Cefaclor
- Cefatrizine

**LOW RISK - Safe to use:**
- **Cefazolin** (NO shared side chain with any penicillin)
- Ceftriaxone
- Cefepime
- Ceftazidime
- Cefuroxime

## Key Points

### **Clinical Approach (2024):**

| Penicillin Allergy Type | Cephalosporin Recommendation |
|------------------------|------------------------------|
| **Non-severe, remote** | Any cephalosporin safe (including same R1) |
| **IgE-mediated (urticaria)** | Avoid cephalosporins with identical R1; use different R1 |
| **Anaphylaxis** | Skin test if same R1 needed; use different R1 preferred |
| **DRESS/SJS/TEN** | **Avoid ALL β-lactams** |

### **Safest Cephalosporins for Penicillin Allergy:**
1. **Cefazolin** - unique side chain (no match)
2. **Ceftriaxone** - different R1
3. **Cefepime** - different R1

### **Clinical Pearls:**
- **Historic "10% cross-reactivity"** was due to cephalosporin contamination with penicillin (outdated)
- **Modern data:** <3% cross-reactivity when R1 side chains differ
- Always check **specific drug** R1 side chain match, not just "penicillin allergy"
