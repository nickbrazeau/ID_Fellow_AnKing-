## Methenamine for UTI Prophylaxis

**Q:** What is methenamine's unique mechanism, when should it be used, and what are its key limitations?

**A:**

**Mechanism:**
- **Prodrug** with no intrinsic antimicrobial activity
- In **acidic urine (pH <5.5)**, methenamine → **formaldehyde**
- Formaldehyde is nonspecific bactericidal agent

**Indication:**
- **Recurrent UTI prophylaxis** (antibiotic-sparing strategy)
- No resistance development (formaldehyde is nonspecific)

**Dosing:**
- **Methenamine hippurate:** 1 g PO BID
- **Methenamine mandelate:** 1 g PO QID

**Requirements for Efficacy:**

| **Requirement** | **Details** |
|-----------------|-------------|
| **Acidic urine** | pH ≤5.5 (maximal effect) |
| **Acidification methods** | Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) 2 g daily, cranberry |
| **Adequate renal function** | GFR >10 mL/min (contraindicated if <10) |

**Key Limitation: Proteus & Pseudomonas**
- **Urease-producing organisms** (Proteus, some Pseudomonas):
  - Hydrolyze urea → ammonia → **alkaline urine** (pH ↑↑)
  - Methenamine cannot convert to formaldehyde
  - **Ineffective for Proteus UTI prophylaxis**

**Contraindications:**
- **Severe renal insufficiency** (GFR <10 mL/min)
- **Severe hepatic insufficiency**
- **Severe dehydration**
- **Urine acidification contraindicated** (e.g., uric acid stones)

**Advantages:**
- Well-tolerated (minimal adverse effects)
- No antibiotic resistance
- Can be used long-term
- 2024 systematic review: Effective, well-tolerated, antibiotic-sparing

**Adverse Effects:**
- GI upset (nausea, vomiting) - take with food
- Rash (rare)
- Bladder irritation (dysuria) with high doses

**Mnemonic: "Methenamine needs Acid, Fails with Proteus"**
- **A**cid (pH <5.5 required)
- **F**ormaldehyde (active metabolite)
- **P**roteus makes alkaline urine → methenamine fails

**Clinical Pearl:**
- Check urine pH to confirm acidification
- Excellent option for patients with recurrent UTIs who want to avoid chronic antibiotics
- Recent reemerging interest as antibiotic stewardship strategy
