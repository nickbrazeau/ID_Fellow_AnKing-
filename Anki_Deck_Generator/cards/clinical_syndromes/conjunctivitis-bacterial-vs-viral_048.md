## Bacterial vs Viral Conjunctivitis

**Q:** What are the key clinical features distinguishing bacterial from viral conjunctivitis, and how should each be treated?

**A:**

**Comparison Table:**

| **Feature** | **Bacterial** | **Viral** |
|-------------|---------------|-----------|
| **Discharge** | **Purulent/mucopurulent** (yellow-white), thick | **Watery** with tearing |
| **Morning** | Eyes matted shut | Mild crusting |
| **Preauricular LAD** | **Rare** | **Common** (tender) |
| **Laterality** | Unilateral → bilateral | Bilateral or unilateral |
| **Symptoms** | Foreign body sensation, burning | **Itching**, URI symptoms |
| **Conjunctival findings** | Papillae | **Follicles** (inferior palpebral) |
| **Duration** | 7-10 days (untreated) | **2-4 weeks** (can last 4-6 weeks) |

**Bacterial Conjunctivitis:**

**Pathogens:**
- **Adults:** Staph aureus (most common), Strep pneumoniae, H. influenzae
- **Children:** H. influenzae, Strep pneumoniae, Moraxella catarrhalis
- **Hyperacute:** Neisseria gonorrhoeae (copious purulent discharge, requires systemic treatment)

**Treatment:**
- Broad-spectrum topical antibiotics x 5-7 days:
  - **Fluoroquinolones:** Moxifloxacin 0.5% or gatifloxacin 0.3% TID-QID
  - **Aminoglycosides:** Tobramycin 0.3% QID
  - **Macrolides:** Azithromycin 1% (DuoTrab) - BID x 2 days, then daily x 5 days
  - **Polymyxin B/trimethoprim:** QID (inexpensive, effective)
- **Contact lens wearers:** Must cover Pseudomonas → fluoroquinolone or aminoglycoside

**Viral Conjunctivitis:**

**Pathogens:**
- **Adenovirus** (most common): Epidemic keratoconjunctivitis (EKC)
- Enteroviruses, HSV, VZV

**Treatment:**
- **Self-limited** (resolves in 10-14 days, can last 4-6 weeks)
- **Supportive care:**
  - Cool compresses
  - Artificial tears
  - Hand hygiene (highly contagious!)
- **NO antibiotics** (unless secondary bacterial infection suspected)

**Special Considerations:**

**Hyperacute Bacterial (Gonococcal):**
- Onset <24 hours, severe purulent discharge, lid swelling
- Risk: Corneal perforation
- Treatment: **Ceftriaxone 1g IM x1** + saline irrigation + topical antibiotics

**When to Refer to Ophthalmology:**
- Vision loss
- Severe pain
- Photophobia (suggests keratitis)
- Corneal involvement
- No improvement after 5-7 days
- Contact lens wearer (risk of keratitis)
- Hyperacute presentation

**Mnemonic: "Bacterial = Pus, Viral = preAuricular LAD"**
- **B**acterial = **P**urulent discharge
- **V**iral = Pre**a**uricular lymphadenopathy

**Clinical Pearl:**
- If you see **follicles** on inferior palpebral conjunctiva → think viral
- If discharge reforms immediately after wiping → bacterial
- Viral conjunctivitis is **highly contagious** → strict handwashing, avoid sharing towels
