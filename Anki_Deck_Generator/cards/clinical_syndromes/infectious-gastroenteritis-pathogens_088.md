## Infectious Gastroenteritis: Major Pathogens

**Q:** What are the major causes of infectious gastroenteritis, their distinguishing features, and approach to diagnosis and treatment?

**A:**

## OVERVIEW

**Epidemiology:**
- **179 million cases/year in US**
- **~1 million hospitalizations**
- **~5,000 deaths** (mostly elderly, immunocompromised)

**Key Distinction:**
- **Non-inflammatory (watery) diarrhea:** Small bowel (enterotoxin-mediated)
- **Inflammatory (bloody/dysenteric) diarrhea:** Large bowel (invasive pathogens)

---

## CLASSIFICATION BY MECHANISM

---

### **Non-Inflammatory (Watery) Diarrhea:**

**Mechanism:** Enterotoxin production → secretory diarrhea

**Pathogens:**
- **Viruses:** Norovirus, rotavirus, adenovirus
- **Bacteria:** *E. coli* (ETEC), *Vibrio cholerae*, *C. perfringens*, *S. aureus* (preformed toxin)
- **Parasites:** *Giardia*, *Cryptosporidium*, *Cyclospora*

**Clinical Features:**
- **Large-volume watery diarrhea**
- **NO blood, NO fecal leukocytes**
- **Minimal fever**
- **Nausea, vomiting, cramping**

---

### **Inflammatory (Bloody/Dysenteric) Diarrhea:**

**Mechanism:** Mucosal invasion → colonic inflammation

**Pathogens:**
- **Bacteria:** *Shigella*, *Salmonella*, *Campylobacter*, STEC (*E. coli* O157:H7), *C. difficile*, *Yersinia*
- **Parasites:** *Entamoeba histolytica*

**Clinical Features:**
- **Small-volume bloody diarrhea**
- **Fecal leukocytes, lactoferrin positive**
- **Fever, tenesmus**
- **Abdominal pain (LLQ common)**

---

## MAJOR VIRAL PATHOGENS

---

### **1. Norovirus (#1 Cause of Gastroenteritis in US)**

**Epidemiology:**
- **#1 cause of acute gastroenteritis** in all age groups (US)
- **19-21 million cases/year in US**
- **Outbreaks:** Cruise ships, nursing homes, schools, hospitals

**Transmission:**
- **Fecal-oral** (contaminated food, water)
- **Very low inoculum** (10-100 virions)
- **Highly contagious** (aerosolized vomit)

**Incubation:** 12-48 hours

**Clinical Features:**
- **Acute onset nausea, vomiting** (prominent)
- **Watery diarrhea**
- **Abdominal cramping**
- **Low-grade fever**
- **Duration:** 1-3 days (self-limited)

**Diagnosis:**
- **Clinical diagnosis** (outbreak setting)
- **Norovirus PCR** (stool) - if needed

**Treatment:**
- **Supportive care** (hydration)
- **No specific antiviral**

**Key Point:** **Norovirus = #1 gastroenteritis** (explosive vomiting, outbreaks, highly contagious)

---

### **2. Rotavirus**

**Epidemiology:**
- **Most common in infants/children** (<5 years)
- **Decreased in US** (due to vaccination - RotaTeq, Rotarix)

**Incubation:** 1-3 days

**Clinical Features:**
- **Watery diarrhea** (severe)
- **Vomiting, fever**
- **Dehydration** (risk in infants)
- **Duration:** 3-8 days

**Diagnosis:**
- **Rotavirus antigen test** (stool)
- **Rotavirus PCR**

**Treatment:**
- **Oral rehydration** (or IV fluids if severe)

**Prevention:**
- **RotaTeq** (pentavalent, 3 doses at 2, 4, 6 months)
- **Rotarix** (monovalent, 2 doses at 2, 4 months)

**Key Point:** **Rotavirus = pediatric gastroenteritis** (vaccine-preventable)

---

## MAJOR BACTERIAL PATHOGENS (Non-Inflammatory)

---

### **3. Enterotoxigenic *E. coli* (ETEC)**

**Epidemiology:**
- **#1 cause of traveler's diarrhea** (30-40%)
- **Developing countries** (contaminated food, water)

**Mechanism:**
- **Heat-labile toxin (LT)** and/or **heat-stable toxin (ST)** → secretory diarrhea

**Incubation:** 1-3 days

**Clinical Features:**
- **Watery diarrhea** (acute onset)
- **Cramping, nausea**
- **Low-grade fever**
- **Duration:** 3-5 days (self-limited)

**Diagnosis:**
- **Clinical** (traveler's diarrhea setting)
- **Culture + PCR** (specialized labs)

**Treatment:**
- **Hydration**
- **Antibiotics** (if moderate-severe):
  - **Azithromycin** 1g PO x1 OR 500 mg PO daily x 3 days (preferred)
  - **Ciprofloxacin** 500 mg PO BID x 3 days (increasing resistance)
- **Loperamide** (antimotility) - if non-bloody diarrhea

**Key Point:** **ETEC = #1 traveler's diarrhea** (watery, self-limited)

---

## MAJOR BACTERIAL PATHOGENS (Inflammatory/Bloody)

---

### **4. *Campylobacter jejuni* (#1 Bacterial Gastroenteritis in Developed Countries)**

**Epidemiology:**
- **#1 bacterial cause** of gastroenteritis in US (1.5 million cases/year)
- **Undercooked poultry** (chicken - #1 source)
- **Raw milk, contaminated water**

**Incubation:** 2-5 days

**Clinical Features:**
- **Bloody diarrhea** (30-50%)
- **Severe abdominal pain** (can mimic appendicitis)
- **Fever, cramping**
- **Duration:** 3-7 days

**Complications:**
- **Guillain-Barré syndrome (GBS)** - #1 infection preceding GBS (20-50% of GBS cases)
- **Reactive arthritis**
- **Bacteremia** (rare, immunocompromised)

**Diagnosis:**
- **Stool culture** (requires special media - Campy agar, 42°C)
- **Stool PCR** (more sensitive)

**Treatment:**
- **Usually self-limited** (supportive care)
- **Antibiotics if severe/immunocompromised:**
  - **Azithromycin** 500 mg PO daily x 3 days (preferred - due to fluoroquinolone resistance)
  - **Ciprofloxacin** (if susceptible)

**Key Point:** ***Campylobacter* = #1 bacterial gastroenteritis** (undercooked chicken, GBS association)

---

### **5. Non-Typhoidal *Salmonella***

**Epidemiology:**
- **1.35 million cases/year in US**
- **Poultry, eggs, reptiles** (turtles, lizards)

**Incubation:** 6-72 hours (usually 12-36h)

**Clinical Features:**
- **Bloody or watery diarrhea**
- **Fever, cramping, nausea/vomiting**
- **Duration:** 4-7 days (self-limited)

**Complications:**
- **Bacteremia** (5-10%, higher in elderly, infants, immunocompromised)
- **Endovascular infection** (mycotic aneurysm, endocarditis - if bacteremia)
- **Osteomyelitis** (sickle cell disease)
- **Chronic carrier state** (1% - chronic gallbladder colonization)

**Diagnosis:**
- **Stool culture**
- **Blood cultures** (if bacteremia)

**Treatment:**
- **Supportive care ONLY** (for uncomplicated gastroenteritis)
- **DO NOT treat with antibiotics** (prolongs carriage, no clinical benefit)

**Antibiotics ONLY if:**
- **Bacteremia**
- **Extraintestinal infection**
- **High risk:** Age <3 months, >65 years, immunocompromised, prosthetic valves/grafts

**Antibiotic Choice (If Indicated):**
- **Ceftriaxone** 1-2g IV q24h OR **Ciprofloxacin** 500 mg PO BID x 7-14 days

**Key Point:** **DO NOT treat uncomplicated Salmonella gastroenteritis** (prolongs carriage, no benefit)

---

### **6. *Shigella***

**Epidemiology:**
- **500,000 cases/year in US**
- **Most contagious bacterial diarrhea** (very low inoculum - 10-100 organisms)
- **Fecal-oral** (person-to-person, contaminated food/water)
- **Daycare outbreaks**, MSM, international travel

**Incubation:** 1-3 days

**Clinical Features:**
- **Bloody diarrhea (dysentery)** - classic
- **Severe cramping, tenesmus**
- **Fever**
- **Duration:** 5-7 days

**Complications:**
- **Hemolytic uremic syndrome (HUS)** - *S. dysenteriae* type 1 (13% risk)
- **Reactive arthritis**
- **Seizures** (children)

**Diagnosis:**
- **Stool culture**
- **Stool PCR**

**Treatment:**
- **ALWAYS treat with antibiotics** (unlike Salmonella)
- **Azithromycin** 500 mg PO daily x 3 days (preferred - due to resistance)
- **Ciprofloxacin** 500 mg PO BID x 3 days (if susceptible)

**Key Point:** **Shigella ALWAYS treat** (unlike Salmonella) - reduces duration, transmission

---

### **7. Shiga Toxin-Producing *E. coli* (STEC) - *E. coli* O157:H7**

**Epidemiology:**
- **265,000 STEC infections/year in US**
- ***E. coli* O157:H7 = most common serotype** (but non-O157 increasing)
- **Undercooked ground beef** (#1), raw milk, contaminated produce (lettuce, spinach)

**Mechanism:**
- **Shiga toxin (Stx1, Stx2)** → Endothelial damage → HUS

**Incubation:** 3-4 days (range 1-10 days)

**Clinical Features:**
- **Severe cramping abdominal pain** (sudden onset)
- **Watery diarrhea → bloody diarrhea** (within 1-2 days)
- **NO fever or low-grade fever** (distinguishes from Shigella)
- **Duration:** 5-10 days

**Complications:**
- **Hemolytic Uremic Syndrome (HUS)** - 5-15% (highest risk in children, elderly)
  - Triad: Microangiopathic hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia, acute kidney injury
  - **Onset:** 5-10 days after diarrhea starts
  - **Leading cause of acute kidney injury in children**

**Diagnosis:**
- **Stool culture** for *E. coli* O157:H7 (sorbitol-negative on MacConkey)
- **Shiga toxin EIA** (stool) - detects toxin (O157 and non-O157)
- **Stool PCR** (for Shiga toxin genes - stx1, stx2)

**Treatment:**
- **Supportive care ONLY** (hydration)
- **DO NOT give antibiotics** - ↑ risk of HUS (by 2-3 fold)
- **DO NOT give antimotility agents** (loperamide) - ↑ HUS risk

**Key Point:** **STEC = NO antibiotics, NO loperamide** (↑ HUS risk)

---

## PARASITIC PATHOGENS

---

### **8. *Giardia lamblia***

**Epidemiology:**
- **Most common parasitic gastroenteritis** in US
- **Contaminated water** (camping, international travel)
- **Daycare outbreaks**

**Incubation:** 1-2 weeks

**Clinical Features:**
- **Watery diarrhea** (chronic if untreated - weeks to months)
- **Bloating, flatulence** (excessive gas)
- **Steatorrhea** (greasy stools)
- **Weight loss** (malabsorption)
- **NO blood, NO fever**

**Diagnosis:**
- **Stool ova & parasites (O&P)** - 3 samples (sensitivity 60-70%)
- **Giardia antigen test** (stool) - more sensitive
- **Stool PCR**

**Treatment:**
- **Metronidazole** 250 mg PO TID x 5-7 days
- **OR Tinidazole** 2g PO x1 (single dose, preferred - better compliance)
- **OR Nitazoxanide** 500 mg PO BID x 3 days

**Key Point:** ***Giardia* = chronic watery diarrhea + bloating** (campers, international travel)

---

### **9. *Cryptosporidium***

**Epidemiology:**
- **Water outbreaks** (chlorine-resistant oocysts)
- **Swimming pools, daycare**
- **AIDS-defining illness** (CD4 <100 - chronic diarrhea)

**Incubation:** 7 days (range 2-10 days)

**Clinical Features:**
- **Watery diarrhea** (profuse in HIV)
- **Cramping, nausea**
- **Duration:** 1-2 weeks (immunocompetent), **chronic** (immunocompromised)

**Diagnosis:**
- **Stool O&P** (modified acid-fast stain)
- **Cryptosporidium antigen test** (stool)
- **Stool PCR**

**Treatment:**
- **Immunocompetent:** Self-limited (supportive care)
- **HIV/immunocompromised:**
  - **Nitazoxanide** 500-1000 mg PO BID x 3-14 days
  - **Immune reconstitution** (ART for HIV - most important)

**Key Point:** ***Cryptosporidium* = chlorine-resistant** (water outbreaks, AIDS-defining)

---

## DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH

---

**Step 1: Clinical Assessment**

**Inflammatory vs Non-Inflammatory:**

| **Feature** | **Non-Inflammatory** | **Inflammatory** |
|-------------|----------------------|------------------|
| **Stool** | Watery, large-volume | Bloody, small-volume |
| **Fever** | Minimal | Common |
| **Fecal leukocytes** | Negative | Positive |
| **Pathogens** | Viruses, ETEC, cholera, *Giardia* | *Campylobacter*, *Shigella*, Salmonella, STEC, *C. difficile* |

---

**Step 2: When to Test?**

**Stool Studies INDICATED if:**
- **Severe illness** (dehydration, hospitalization)
- **Bloody diarrhea**
- **Fever >38.5°C**
- **Duration >7 days**
- **Immunocompromised**
- **Outbreak investigation**
- **Recent antibiotic use** (test for *C. difficile*)

**Stool Studies NOT Needed if:**
- **Mild, watery diarrhea <3 days** (likely viral, self-limited)
- **Vomiting predominant** (likely norovirus)

---

**Step 3: Stool Testing**

**Routine Panel:**
- **Stool culture** (Salmonella, Shigella, Campylobacter)
- **Shiga toxin EIA** or **STEC culture** (if bloody diarrhea)
- ***C. difficile* PCR** (if recent antibiotics or healthcare exposure)

**Multiplex PCR Panel (If Available):**
- **Gastrointestinal PCR panel** (detects 20+ pathogens simultaneously)
  - Bacteria: Campylobacter, Salmonella, Shigella, STEC, C. difficile, Yersinia
  - Viruses: Norovirus, rotavirus, adenovirus
  - Parasites: Giardia, Cryptosporidium, Entamoeba

**Additional Tests (If Indicated):**
- **Stool O&P** (if chronic diarrhea, travel, immunocompromised)
- **Fecal leukocytes** or **lactoferrin** (inflammatory diarrhea)

---

## TREATMENT PRINCIPLES

---

**1. Hydration (CORNERSTONE):**
- **Oral rehydration solution (ORS)** - preferred
- **IV fluids** - if severe dehydration

---

**2. Antibiotics:**

**DO NOT Treat:**
- **Uncomplicated Salmonella gastroenteritis** (prolongs carriage)
- **STEC** (↑ HUS risk)
- **Viral gastroenteritis** (no benefit)

**ALWAYS Treat:**
- ***Shigella*** (reduces duration, transmission)
- ***C. difficile*** (fidaxomicin or vancomycin)
- **Traveler's diarrhea (moderate-severe)** (azithromycin, ciprofloxacin)

**Treat if Severe/Immunocompromised:**
- ***Campylobacter*** (azithromycin)
- **Salmonella bacteremia** (ceftriaxone, ciprofloxacin)

---

**3. Antimotility Agents (Loperamide):**

**Safe if:**
- **Non-bloody, watery diarrhea**
- **NO fever**
- **NO STEC** (do NOT use if STEC suspected)

**Contraindicated if:**
- **Bloody diarrhea**
- **High fever**
- ***C. difficile* colitis**
- **STEC** (↑ HUS risk)

---

**Mnemonic: "Norovirus = Vomiting + Outbreaks"**
- **N**orovirus = #1 gastroenteritis
- **V**omiting (prominent)
- **O**utbreaks (cruise ships, nursing homes)

**Mnemonic: "Campylobacter = Chicken + GBS"**
- **C**hicken (undercooked poultry)
- **GBS** (Guillain-Barré syndrome - #1 preceding infection)

**Mnemonic: "Salmonella = DON'T Treat (Gastroenteritis)"**
- **D**on't treat uncomplicated gastroenteritis
- **T**reat ONLY if bacteremia, extraintestinal, high-risk

**Mnemonic: "Shigella = ALWAYS Treat"**
- **A**lways treat (reduces duration, transmission)
- **A**zithromycin preferred (resistance)

**Mnemonic: "STEC = NO Antibiotics, NO Loperamide"**
- **N**o antibiotics (↑ HUS risk 2-3 fold)
- **N**o loperamide (↑ HUS risk)

**Clinical Pearls:**
- **Norovirus = #1 gastroenteritis** (all ages), explosive vomiting, outbreaks
- **ETEC = #1 traveler's diarrhea** (30-40%)
- ***Campylobacter* = #1 bacterial gastroenteritis** (developed countries), chicken, GBS
- **DO NOT treat uncomplicated Salmonella gastroenteritis** (prolongs carriage)
- **ALWAYS treat Shigella** (azithromycin - reduces duration, transmission)
- **STEC: NO antibiotics, NO loperamide** (↑ HUS risk)
- **HUS triad:** Microangiopathic hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia, AKI
- ***Giardia*:** Chronic watery diarrhea + bloating (campers, international travel)
- **Multiplex GI PCR detects 20+ pathogens** (more sensitive than culture)
- **Stool testing NOT needed** if mild watery diarrhea <3 days (likely viral)
- **Loperamide contraindicated** if bloody diarrhea, fever, C. difficile, STEC
