## Periorbital (Preseptal) vs Orbital (Postseptal) Cellulitis

**Q:** What are the key clinical features distinguishing periorbital from orbital cellulitis, and how should each be managed?

**A:**

**Anatomy Key:**
- **Orbital septum:** Fibrous membrane extending from periosteum to tarsal plates
- **Preseptal (periorbital):** Infection **anterior** to septum (eyelid/surrounding skin only)
- **Postseptal (orbital):** Infection **posterior** to septum (involves orbital contents)

**Comparison Table:**

| **Feature** | **Periorbital (Preseptal)** | **Orbital (Postseptal)** |
|-------------|------------------------------|--------------------------|
| **Location** | Anterior to orbital septum | Posterior to orbital septum |
| **Eyelid swelling/erythema** | ✅ Yes (localized) | ✅ Yes |
| **Visual acuity** | **Normal** | **Decreased** |
| **Proptosis** | ❌ Absent | ✅ **Present** |
| **Ophthalmoplegia** (painful EOM) | ❌ Absent | ✅ **Present** |
| **Pain with eye movement** | ❌ Absent | ✅ **Present** |
| **Chemosis** (conjunctival edema) | Rare | ✅ Common |
| **Fever/systemic illness** | Mild or absent | Often present |
| **Imaging** | Usually not needed | **CT or MRI required** |
| **Hospitalization** | Outpatient (mild cases) | **Admission required** |

**Key Distinguishing Features (3 P's):**
1. **Proptosis** (eye bulging)
2. **Pain with eye movement**
3. **Paralysis of extraocular muscles** (ophthalmoplegia)

**If ANY of these present → Orbital cellulitis**

---

**Etiology:**

**Periorbital (Preseptal) Cellulitis:**
- **Causes:**
  - Trauma/insect bite (most common in adults)
  - Spread from facial cellulitis
  - Secondary to sinusitis (less common)
- **Pathogens:** Staph aureus (including MRSA), Strep pyogenes

**Orbital (Postseptal) Cellulitis:**
- **Causes:**
  - **Acute sinusitis** (most common - 90% of cases, especially **ethmoid sinusitis**)
  - Dental infection
  - Trauma (penetrating injury)
  - Post-surgical
- **Pathogens:**
  - **Children:** Strep pneumoniae, Strep pyogenes, Staph aureus, H. influenzae (rare post-Hib vaccine), anaerobes
  - **Adults:** Staph aureus (including MRSA), Strep species, anaerobes (if sinusitis-related)

---

**Imaging:**

**When to Image:**
- **Periorbital:** Not routinely needed if classic presentation + no orbital signs
- **Orbital:** **Always image** (contrast-enhanced CT orbits/sinuses or MRI)
- **If uncertain:** Image (treat as orbital if doubt exists)

**CT Findings:**

| **Finding** | **Periorbital** | **Orbital** |
|-------------|-----------------|-------------|
| Soft tissue swelling | Anterior to septum | Posterior to septum |
| Proptosis | Absent | Present |
| Extraocular muscles | Normal | Edematous/thickened |
| Orbital fat | Normal | Stranding/edema |
| Sinusitis | May be absent | Usually present (ethmoid > maxillary) |
| Abscess | Rare | May be present (subperiosteal or orbital) |

---

**Treatment:**

**Periorbital (Preseptal) Cellulitis:**

**Mild Cases (Outpatient):**
- **Oral antibiotics x 7-10 days:**
  - **First-line:** Clindamycin 300-450 mg PO TID (covers MRSA)
  - **Alternatives:** Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875 mg PO BID, cephalexin 500 mg PO QID
  - **If MRSA suspected:** TMP-SMX + cephalexin or clindamycin monotherapy

**High-Risk (Admission + IV):**
- Age <1 year, immunocompromised, no immunization, failed outpatient therapy
- **IV options:** Cefuroxime, ceftriaxone, ampicillin-sulbactam, piperacillin-tazobactam
- **Add vancomycin if MRSA suspected**

**Orbital (Postseptal) Cellulitis:**

**Always Admit + IV Antibiotics:**

**Empiric Regimen (Broad-Spectrum):**
- **Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h**
  - (covers MRSA + Strep pneumoniae)
- PLUS
- **Ceftriaxone 2g IV q24h** or **cefotaxime 2g IV q6h**
  - (covers Strep, H. influenzae)
- PLUS (if sinusitis-related or suspected anaerobes):
- **Metronidazole 500 mg IV q8h** or **ampicillin-sulbactam 3g IV q6h**

**Penicillin Allergy:**
- Vancomycin + ciprofloxacin or levofloxacin + metronidazole

**Duration:** 2-3 weeks total (IV until improved, then switch to PO)

---

**Surgical Indications (Orbital Cellulitis):**
- Orbital abscess (>10 mm)
- Subperiosteal abscess
- Worsening despite 24-48h IV antibiotics
- Vision deterioration
- Acute vision loss
- Large frontal sinusitis with risk of intracranial extension

**Complications (Orbital Cellulitis):**
- **Vision loss** (optic nerve compression, central retinal artery occlusion)
- **Cavernous sinus thrombosis**
- **Intracranial extension:** Meningitis, epidural abscess, brain abscess
- **Mortality:** ~5-10% if untreated

---

**Mnemonic: "3 P's = Postseptal (Orbital)"**
- **P**roptosis
- **P**ain with eye movement
- **P**aralysis of EOMs (ophthalmoplegia)

**If ANY of these → It's ORBITAL, not periorbital!**

**Clinical Pearls:**
- **Most important distinguishing feature:** Proptosis + painful ophthalmoplegia
- **When in doubt, image** - treat as orbital cellulitis if uncertain
- Orbital cellulitis = ophthalmologic + ENT emergency
- Most orbital cellulitis arises from **ethmoid sinusitis** (medial spread)
- **Never discharge orbital cellulitis** - all need admission + IV antibiotics
- If vision acutely worsens → emergent surgical decompression (orbital compartment syndrome)
