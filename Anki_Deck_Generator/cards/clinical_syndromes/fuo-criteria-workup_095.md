## Question

What defines FUO and how is it worked up? Use the **"3 Weeks + 3 Visits (or 3 Days Inpatient) + No Diagnosis"** criteria.

## Answer

### **FUO Criteria (Petersdorf & Beeson, Updated 1991):**

**Classic FUO:**
- **Fever ≥38.3°C (101°F)** on multiple occasions
- **Duration ≥3 weeks**
- **No diagnosis after 3 outpatient visits OR 3 days inpatient investigation**

**Subtypes:**
- **Nosocomial FUO:** Hospitalized ≥24h, no infection on admission
- **Neutropenic FUO:** ANC <500, no diagnosis after 3 days despite cultures
- **HIV-associated FUO:** HIV+ with fever ≥3 weeks (outpatient) or ≥3 days (inpatient)

### **Diagnostic Approach:**

**Phase 1: Comprehensive History + Exam + Basic Labs**
- **History:** Travel, exposures, medications, occupational, sexual, animal contact
- **Exam:** Repeated exams (rash, lymphadenopathy, heart murmur, temporal arteries)
- **Labs:** CBC, CMP, LFTs, ESR/CRP, UA, blood cultures (×3), CXR

**Phase 2: Focused Imaging**
- **CT chest/abdomen/pelvis** with contrast
- **PET-CT** (if CT negative, detects malignancy, vasculitis, infection)

**Phase 3: Targeted Testing**
- **Rheumatologic:** ANA, RF, ANCA (vasculitis), temporal artery biopsy (if age >50 + headache + ↑ESR)
- **Infectious:** HIV, TB testing (IGRA, sputum), fungal serologies, CMV/EBV PCR
- **Malignancy:** Age-appropriate cancer screening, bone marrow biopsy

### **Common Causes by Category:**

| Category | Frequency | Examples |
|----------|-----------|----------|
| **Infections** | **30-40%** | TB, endocarditis, abscesses, osteomyelitis |
| **Malignancies** | **20-30%** | Lymphoma, leukemia, renal cell carcinoma |
| **Autoimmune/Inflammatory** | **10-20%** | Giant cell arteritis, Still's disease, sarcoidosis |
| **Miscellaneous** | **10-20%** | Drug fever, VTE, factitious fever |
| **Undiagnosed** | **10-20%** | Often resolve spontaneously |

## Key Points

### **Red Flags by Age:**
- **Age >50 + ↑ESR + headache:** **Temporal arteritis** (biopsy temporal artery)
- **Age >50 + weight loss:** **Malignancy** (lymphoma, renal cell CA)
- **Young adult + rash + arthritis:** **Still's disease** (salmon-colored rash, very high ferritin)

### **High-Yield Diagnoses Not to Miss:**
- **Giant cell arteritis** (age >50, headache, jaw claudication, ↑ESR >100)
- **Endocarditis** (repeat TEE if high suspicion)
- **Occult abscess** (intra-abdominal, liver, splenic)
- **Tuberculosis** (most common infectious cause worldwide)
- **Lymphoma** (especially if weight loss, night sweats)

### **Clinical Pearls:**
- **PET-CT** high yield if CT negative (detects malignancy, vasculitis)
- **Drug fever:** Stop all non-essential meds (diagnosis of exclusion)
- **Temporal artery biopsy** if age >50 + ↑ESR (even without headache)
- **Bone marrow biopsy** if cytopenias or undifferentiated symptoms
- **10-20% remain undiagnosed** (many resolve spontaneously)
