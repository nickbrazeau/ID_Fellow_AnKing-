## Question

What are key zoonotic infections by animal exposure? Use the **"Animal Contact → Pathogen → Treatment"** framework.

## Answer

### **Zoonoses by Animal Exposure:**

| Animal | Disease | Pathogen | Presentation | Treatment |
|--------|---------|----------|--------------|-----------|
| **Dogs/Bats/Raccoons** | **Rabies** | Rabies virus (Lyssavirus) | Prodrome → **acute encephalitis** (hydrophobia, aerophobia) → death | **Post-exposure prophylaxis:** HRIG + vaccine (if not pre-vaccinated) |
| **Cattle/Goats/Sheep** | **Brucellosis** | *Brucella* spp. | **Undulant fever**, arthralgia, hepatosplenomegaly, back pain | **Doxycycline 100mg BID + rifampin 600mg daily × 6 weeks** |
| **Cattle/Sheep** | **Q Fever** | *Coxiella burnetii* | **Fever, pneumonia**, hepatitis, endocarditis (chronic) | **Doxycycline 100mg BID × 14 days** (acute); × 18 months (chronic endocarditis) |
| **Rodents/Water** | **Leptospirosis** | *Leptospira* spp. | **Biphasic fever**, conjunctival suffusion, **Weil disease** (jaundice, AKI, hemorrhage) | **Penicillin G 1.5 MU IV q6h** (severe) OR **doxycycline 100mg BID** (mild) |
| **Cats** | **Cat Scratch Disease** | *Bartonella henselae* | Regional lymphadenopathy (axillary), fever | **Azithromycin 500mg × 1 → 250mg daily × 4 days** OR observe (self-limited) |
| **Rabbits/Rodents** | **Tularemia** | *Francisella tularensis* | **Ulceroglandular** (skin ulcer + lymphadenopathy), **typhoidal** (fever, hepatosplenomegaly) | **Streptomycin** OR **gentamicin** OR **doxycycline** |
| **Birds (Parrots)** | **Psittacosis** | *Chlamydia psittaci* | **Atypical pneumonia**, fever, hepatosplenomegaly, relative bradycardia | **Doxycycline 100mg BID × 10-14 days** |

### **Rabies (Always Fatal Once Symptomatic):**

**Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP):**

| Vaccination Status | PEP Regimen |
|-------------------|-------------|
| **Not vaccinated** | **HRIG 20 IU/kg** (infiltrate wound + IM) + **Rabies vaccine** (days 0, 3, 7, 14) |
| **Pre-vaccinated** | **Rabies vaccine only** (days 0, 3) - **NO HRIG** |

**High-Risk Exposures:**
- **Bats** (even without bite), raccoons, skunks, foxes, coyotes
- **Dogs/cats** in developing countries

## Key Points

### **Brucellosis:**
- **Occupational:** Farmers, slaughterhouse workers, veterinarians
- **Transmission:** Unpasteurized dairy, direct contact with infected animals
- **"Undulant fever"** (relapsing/remitting fever pattern)
- **Treatment:** **Doxycycline + rifampin × 6 weeks** (monotherapy high relapse)

### **Q Fever:**
- **No tick vector** (inhalation of aerosols from animals)
- **Acute:** Fever, pneumonia, hepatitis
- **Chronic (1% of cases):** **Endocarditis** (especially prosthetic valves)
- **Treatment:** Doxy × 14 days (acute), doxy + hydroxychloroquine × 18 months (chronic endocarditis)

### **Leptospirosis:**
- **Transmission:** Exposure to water/soil contaminated with rodent urine (flooding, recreational water)
- **Biphasic:** Acute febrile → immune phase
- **Weil disease:** Severe form (jaundice, AKI, pulmonary hemorrhage)
- **Conjunctival suffusion** (red eyes without discharge) = classic sign

### **Clinical Pearls:**
- **Rabies:** ALWAYS fatal once symptomatic → PEP critical (HRIG + vaccine if not pre-vaccinated)
- **Brucellosis:** Doxycycline + rifampin × 6 weeks (relapsing fever, back pain)
- **Q fever:** Pneumonia + no tick exposure, doxycycline (acute), prolonged therapy (chronic endocarditis)
- **Leptospirosis:** Conjunctival suffusion, Weil disease (jaundice + AKI)
- **Doxycycline** treats most zoonoses (except rabies)
