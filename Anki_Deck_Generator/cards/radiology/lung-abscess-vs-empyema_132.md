## Lung Abscess vs Empyema - CT Differentiation

**Q:** How do you distinguish lung abscess from empyema on CT imaging using the "angle with chest wall" and other radiologic features?

**A:**

## KEY DIFFERENTIATING FEATURES

**Mnemonic: "ANGLE SPLITS"**

**A**cute angle (<90°) = **Abscess** (intraparenchymal)
**N**odular, round/spherical shape = Abscess
**G**ets obtuse angle (>90°) = **Empyema** (pleural space)
**L**enticular (lens-shaped) = Empyema
**E**qual air-fluid level all positions = Abscess

**S**plit pleura sign = Empyema (enhanced pleural layers)
**P**leural layers thin, smooth = Empyema
**L**ung fixed (doesn't move) = Abscess
**I**rregular thick wall (>4mm) = Abscess
**T**hin wall, smooth = Empyema
**S**pace-occupying (moves with respiration) = Empyema

---

## ANGLE WITH CHEST WALL (Most Important)

**Lung Abscess:**
- **Acute angle (<90°)** with pleura
- **Spherical/round shape**
- **Intraparenchymal origin** (within lung tissue)

**Empyema:**
- **Obtuse angle (>90°)** with pleura
- **Lenticular (lens-shaped)** or crescentic
- **Pleural space location** (between parietal and visceral pleura)

**Key Point:** **Acute angle = abscess** | **Obtuse angle = empyema**

---

## COMPARATIVE TABLE

| **Feature** | **Lung Abscess** | **Empyema** |
|-------------|------------------|-------------|
| **Location** | **Intraparenchymal** | **Pleural space** |
| **Angle with chest wall** | **Acute (<90°)** | **Obtuse (>90°)** |
| **Shape** | **Round/spherical** | **Lenticular (lens-shaped)** |
| **Wall thickness** | **Thick (>4mm), irregular** | **Thin, smooth (pleural layers)** |
| **Inner lining** | **Shaggy** (necrotic debris) | **Smooth** |
| **Air-fluid level** | **Equal length all positions** | **Variable with position** |
| **Split pleura sign** | **Absent** | **Present** (enhanced visceral + parietal pleura) |
| **Moves with respiration** | **No** (fixed in lung) | **Yes** (in pleural space) |
| **Surrounding tissue** | **Consolidation/ground-glass** | **Compressed lung** |
| **Distribution** | **Dependent segments** (aspiration) | **Dependent pleural space** |

---

## SPLIT PLEURA SIGN (Empyema)

**Definition:** **"Enhanced visceral and parietal pleural layers separated by fluid"**

**Appearance on Contrast CT:**
- **Two distinct enhanced lines** (visceral + parietal pleura)
- **Hypodense fluid collection** between them
- **Thickened pleural layers** (>2mm each)

**Significance:**
- **Highly specific for empyema** (>90%)
- **Indicates pleural space infection** (not just sterile effusion)
- **Suggests need for drainage** (chest tube, VATS)

**Key Point:** **Split pleura sign = empyema** (requires pleural drainage)

---

## AIR-FLUID LEVEL CHARACTERISTICS

**Lung Abscess:**
- **Equal length in all body positions** (supine, prone, decubitus)
- **Round/spherical cavity**
- **Thick irregular wall**

**Empyema:**
- **Variable length with body position** (fluid shifts in pleural space)
- **Conforms to pleural space shape** (lenticular)
- **Thin smooth wall**

**Key Point:** **Fixed air-fluid level = abscess** | **Shifting air-fluid level = empyema**

---

## WALL CHARACTERISTICS

**Lung Abscess Wall:**
- **Thick (>4mm)** - often 5-15mm
- **Irregular/shaggy inner surface** (necrotic debris)
- **Enhances with contrast** (vascularized inflammatory tissue)
- **Surrounded by consolidation** or ground-glass opacity

**Empyema Wall:**
- **Thin (<4mm)** - typically 1-3mm
- **Smooth inner surface**
- **Two distinct pleural layers** (split pleura sign)
- **No surrounding parenchymal consolidation** (compresses adjacent lung)

**Key Point:** **Thick irregular wall = abscess** | **Thin smooth wall = empyema**

---

## CLINICAL CONTEXT

**Lung Abscess:**
- **Risk factors:** Aspiration (EtOH, seizure, dysphagia), poor dentition
- **Microbiology:** Anaerobes (foul-smelling sputum), S. aureus, Klebsiella
- **Location:** Posterior segments (RUL, RLL superior segments, RML)
- **Treatment:** Prolonged antibiotics (4-6 weeks), postural drainage

**Empyema:**
- **Risk factors:** Parapneumonic effusion (complication of pneumonia), post-surgical
- **Microbiology:** Similar to adjacent pneumonia (Strep pneumo, Staph aureus, anaerobes)
- **Classification:** Simple parapneumonic → complicated → empyema
- **Treatment:** Drainage (chest tube, VATS, decortication) + antibiotics

**Key Point:** **Abscess = prolonged antibiotics** | **Empyema = drainage + antibiotics**

---

## PARAPNEUMONIC EFFUSION SPECTRUM

**Mnemonic: "SIMPLE → COMPLICATED → EMPYEMA"**

1. **Simple Parapneumonic Effusion:**
   - **Sterile** (reactive)
   - **pH >7.20, glucose >60, LDH <1000**
   - **Treatment:** Antibiotics alone (no drainage needed)

2. **Complicated Parapneumonic Effusion:**
   - **Bacteria present but not frankly purulent**
   - **pH <7.20, glucose <60, LDH >1000**
   - **Loculated** (fibrin septations)
   - **Treatment:** Drainage (chest tube) + antibiotics

3. **Empyema:**
   - **Frank pus** (thick, turbid)
   - **Positive Gram stain/culture**
   - **pH <7.0, glucose <40, LDH >1000**
   - **Treatment:** Drainage (chest tube, VATS, decortication) + antibiotics

**Key Point:** **pH <7.20 or glucose <60 = drainage indicated** (complicated effusion or empyema)

---

## DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH

**When to Consider Empyema:**
1. **Pleural effusion + fever + leukocytosis**
2. **Pneumonia not responding to antibiotics**
3. **Persistent fever after 3-5 days of treatment**
4. **Pleural fluid with positive Gram stain or culture**

**Thoracentesis Criteria for Drainage:**
- **pH <7.20** (most important)
- **Glucose <60 mg/dL**
- **LDH >1000 U/L**
- **Positive Gram stain or culture**
- **Loculated effusion on imaging**

**Key Point:** **Thoracentesis mandatory** to differentiate simple vs complicated parapneumonic effusion

---

## TREATMENT DIFFERENCES

**Lung Abscess:**
- **Antibiotics:** Ampicillin-sulbactam 3g IV q6h OR piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5g IV q6h
- **Duration:** 4-6 weeks (until cavity <2cm or resolves)
- **Drainage:** Only if not responding (percutaneous catheter for cavity >5cm)
- **Surgery:** Rarely needed (lobectomy if refractory)

**Empyema:**
- **Antibiotics:** Similar to lung abscess (anaerobic coverage)
- **Duration:** 2-4 weeks (shorter than abscess)
- **Drainage:** **Always required** (chest tube, VATS, decortication)
- **Surgery:** VATS decortication if organized (Stage III), open thoracotomy if chronic

**Key Point:** **Abscess = antibiotics first** | **Empyema = drainage required**

---

**Clinical Pearls:**
- **Acute angle (<90°) with chest wall = lung abscess** (intraparenchymal)
- **Obtuse angle (>90°) = empyema** (pleural space)
- **Split pleura sign = empyema** (enhanced visceral + parietal pleura layers)
- **Lenticular (lens-shaped) = empyema** | **Spherical/round = abscess**
- **Thick irregular wall (>4mm) = abscess** | **Thin smooth wall = empyema**
- **Fixed air-fluid level = abscess** | **Shifting air-fluid level = empyema**
- **Empyema always requires drainage** (chest tube, VATS, surgery)
- **Abscess usually responds to antibiotics alone** (drainage only if refractory)
- **pH <7.20 or glucose <60 on thoracentesis = drainage indicated**
- **Anaerobic coverage essential for both** (foul-smelling if anaerobes)
