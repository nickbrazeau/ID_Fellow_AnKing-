## Psoas Abscess

**Q:** What are the characteristic imaging features of psoas abscess on CT and MRI, what are the primary vs secondary causes, and when is surgical drainage indicated?

**A:**

## DEFINITION

**Psoas Abscess:** **"Collection of pus within the psoas muscle or iliopsoas compartment"**

**CT/MRI Hallmark:** **Rim-enhancing fluid collection within psoas muscle**

**Key Point:** **Psoas abscess = primary (hematogenous) or secondary (contiguous spread)**

---

## ANATOMY

**Psoas Muscle:**
- **Origin:** T12-L5 vertebral bodies and transverse processes
- **Insertion:** Lesser trochanter of femur
- **Function:** Hip flexion
- **Location:** Retroperitoneal (posterior to kidney, anterior to spine)

**Iliopsoas:**
- **Psoas major + iliacus** (merge at L5-S1)
- **Compartment:** Iliopsoas fascia (contains psoas + iliacus muscles)

**Key Point:** **Psoas is retroperitoneal** (not intra-abdominal - affects surgical approach)

---

## PRIMARY vs SECONDARY PSOAS ABSCESS

**Primary Psoas Abscess (20-30%):**
- **Hematogenous seeding** (bacteremia)
- **No identifiable adjacent source**
- **More common in:** Immunocompromised, IVDU, diabetes, HIV
- **Organisms:** S. aureus (90%)

**Secondary Psoas Abscess (70-80%):**
- **Contiguous spread** from adjacent infection
- **Sources:**
  1. **Vertebral osteomyelitis/discitis** (40-50% - most common)
  2. **Intra-abdominal infection** (20-30% - appendicitis, diverticulitis, Crohn's)
  3. **Renal/perinephric abscess** (10-15%)
  4. **Pelvic infection** (5-10% - tubo-ovarian abscess, pelvic abscess)
- **Organisms:** Polymicrobial (E. coli, Enterococcus, Bacteroides)

**Key Point:** **Secondary (70-80%) = contiguous spread** | **Primary (20-30%) = hematogenous S. aureus**

---

## PATHOPHYSIOLOGY

**Formation:**

**Mnemonic: "HEMATOGENOUS-CONTIGUOUS-DRAIN"**

**Primary (Hematogenous):**
**H**ematogenous (bacteremia seeds psoas muscle)
**E**ndocarditis (source of septic emboli)
**M**uscle vasculature (rich blood supply)
**A**bscess formation (localized infection)
**T**ransient bacteremia (dental, skin)
**O**rigin distant (no adjacent source)
**G**erminate in muscle
**E**mbolic (septic)
**N**o adjacent focus
**O**rganisms (S. aureus 90%)
**U**sually immunocompromised
**S**eeds psoas muscle

**Secondary (Contiguous):**
**C**ontiguous spread (from adjacent infection)
**O**steomyelitis (vertebral - most common secondary source, 40-50%)
**N**eighboring organ (kidney, bowel, pelvis)
**T**racks down psoas
**I**BD (Crohn's disease - fistula to psoas)
**G**astrointestinal (appendicitis, diverticulitis)
**U**terine/adnexal (tubo-ovarian abscess)
**O**rigin adjacent (identifiable source)
**U**sually polymicrobial
**S**pread from spine (discitis)

**D**rains down (tracks inferiorly along psoas)
**R**etroperitoneal (confined by fascia)
**A**long muscle (extends down to thigh)
**I**liopsoas compartment (contained)
**N**arrow space (fascia limits spread laterally)

**Key Point:** **Vertebral osteomyelitis = #1 secondary cause** (40-50% of secondary)

---

## CLINICAL PRESENTATION

**Classic Triad (Limp-Pain-Fever) - Only 30% Have All Three:**
1. **Hip/flank pain** (90-95%)
2. **Fever** (60-100%)
3. **Limp** or **inability to extend hip** (20-30%)

**Additional Symptoms:**
- **Back pain** (if vertebral source)
- **Abdominal pain** (if GI source)
- **Weight loss** (chronic)
- **Flexed hip posture** (psoas sign - pain with hip extension)

**Physical Exam:**
- **Psoas sign** (pain with hip extension - stretches inflamed psoas)
- **Flexed, externally rotated hip** (position of comfort)
- **Tender flank/lower abdomen**
- **Fever** (60-100%)

**Key Point:** **Hip/flank pain + fever + psoas sign = psoas abscess** (only 30% have classic triad)

---

## CT FINDINGS (Preferred Modality)

**CT = Gold Standard for Psoas Abscess**

**Advantages:**
- **Fast, widely available**
- **Excellent retroperitoneal detail**
- **Guides percutaneous drainage**
- **Detects adjacent pathology** (spine, kidney, bowel)

**Hallmark: Rim-Enhancing Fluid Collection in Psoas Muscle**

---

### **CT WITH CONTRAST FINDINGS:**

**Mnemonic: "RIM-ENHANCE-PSOAS-HYPODENSE"**

**Abscess:**
- **Hypodense fluid collection** (10-30 HU - pus, necrosis)
- **Rim enhancement** (abscess wall - vascularized granulation tissue)
- **Gas bubbles** (if gas-forming organism or bowel communication - 10-20%)
- **Loculated** (septations within abscess)

**R**im enhancement (abscess wall)
**I**nfiltration of psoas
**M**uscle enlarged (swollen psoas)

**E**nhancing rim (granulation tissue)
**N**on-enhancing center (pus)
**H**ypodense fluid (10-30 HU)
**A**bscess cavity
**N**ecrotic center
**C**avity fluid (pus)
**E**nlarged psoas muscle

**P**soas muscle (enlarged, ill-defined)
**S**wollen muscle
**O**bliteration of fat planes (retroperitoneal fat stranding)
**A**bscess within muscle
**S**tranding (adjacent fat)

**H**ypodense collection (low attenuation)
**Y**ielding to fluid (pus)
**P**us collection (central)
**O**bliterated margins
**D**ense rim (enhancing wall)
**E**nhancing granulation tissue
**N**ecrosis (central low attenuation)
**S**eptations (loculated)
**E**nlargement (swollen psoas)

**Additional Findings:**
- **Psoas muscle enlargement** (swollen compared to contralateral)
- **Obliteration of fat planes** (retroperitoneal fat stranding)
- **Adjacent pathology:**
  - **Vertebral osteomyelitis/discitis** (if secondary)
  - **Renal abscess/pyelonephritis**
  - **Bowel wall thickening** (Crohn's, diverticulitis)
  - **Appendicitis** (inflamed appendix)

**Key Point:** **Rim-enhancing hypodense collection in psoas = abscess** (CT with contrast)

---

### **UNENHANCED CT:**

- **Hypodense mass** in psoas (fluid attenuation)
- **Loss of psoas margin** (ill-defined muscle)
- **Gas bubbles** (if present - suggests gas-forming organism)

**Limitation:** Cannot distinguish abscess from phlegmon without contrast

**Key Point:** **Contrast essential** to confirm abscess (rim enhancement)

---

## MRI FINDINGS

**MRI Advantages:**
- **Superior soft tissue detail**
- **Better for spinal assessment** (if vertebral source suspected)
- **No radiation** (pediatrics, pregnancy)
- **DWI** (confirms purulent nature)

**Hallmark: T2 Hyperintense Collection with Rim Enhancement**

---

### **T1-WEIGHTED:**

- **Hypointense fluid collection** (pus - low signal)
- **Enlarged psoas muscle** (ill-defined margins)
- **Hypointense spine** (if vertebral osteomyelitis - marrow edema)

**Key Point:** **T1 shows abscess as dark fluid** in psoas

---

### **T2/STIR:**

- **Hyperintense fluid collection** (very bright - pus, edema)
- **Psoas muscle edema** (hyperintense striated pattern)
- **Adjacent soft tissue edema** (retroperitoneal fat stranding)
- **Vertebral edema** (if discitis/osteomyelitis - T2 bright marrow)

**Key Point:** **T2 shows abscess as very bright** fluid collection

---

### **POST-CONTRAST T1 FAT-SATURATED:**

- **Rim enhancement** (abscess wall)
- **Central non-enhancing** (purulent fluid)
- **Psoas muscle enhancement** (inflamed muscle)
- **Adjacent enhancement:**
  - **Vertebral body/disc** (if discitis)
  - **Renal parenchyma** (if pyelonephritis)
  - **Bowel wall** (if Crohn's/diverticulitis)

**Key Point:** **Rim enhancement + central non-enhancing = abscess**

---

### **DWI/ADC:**

- **DWI hyperintense** (restricted diffusion - purulent abscess)
- **ADC hypointense** (low ADC values)
- **Confirms purulent nature** (80-100% specific)

**Key Point:** **DWI bright + ADC dark = purulent abscess** (confirms diagnosis)

---

## ULTRASOUND FINDINGS (Limited Utility)

**Challenges:**
- **Retroperitoneal location** (deep, obscured by bowel gas)
- **Body habitus** (obese patients difficult)

**Findings (If Visualized):**
- **Hypoechoic to anechoic collection** (fluid)
- **Posterior shadowing** (if gas present)
- **Enlarged psoas muscle**

**Key Point:** **CT/MRI superior** to US (psoas is retroperitoneal, deep)

---

## ORGANISMS

**Primary Psoas Abscess (Hematogenous):**
- ***Staphylococcus aureus*** (90% - MRSA or MSSA)
- **Streptococcus** species (5-10%)

**Secondary Psoas Abscess (Contiguous Spread):**

**Vertebral Osteomyelitis Source:**
- ***S. aureus*** (60-70%)
- ***M. tuberculosis*** (5-10% - TB psoas "cold abscess")

**GI Source (Crohn's, Appendicitis, Diverticulitis):**
- **Polymicrobial** (80%)
- ***E. coli*** (most common Gram-negative)
- **Enterococcus** species
- **Anaerobes** (Bacteroides, Fusobacterium)
- **Streptococcus** species

**Renal Source:**
- ***E. coli*** (most common)
- **Klebsiella**
- **Proteus**

**Key Point:** **Primary = S. aureus (90%)** | **Secondary = polymicrobial (E. coli, Enterococcus, anaerobes)**

---

## DIAGNOSIS

**Imaging:**
- **CT with contrast** (preferred - 90-95% sensitive)
- **MRI with contrast + DWI** (if CT contraindicated or spinal assessment needed)

**Labs:**
- **WBC elevated** (leukocytosis 70-80%)
- **ESR/CRP elevated** (90-95%)
- **Blood cultures** (positive 30-60%)

**Drainage with Culture:**
- **CT-guided aspiration** or **surgical drainage**
- **Gram stain + culture + AFB stain** (if TB suspected)

**Key Point:** **CT with contrast + drainage with culture** (diagnostic and therapeutic)

---

## TREATMENT

**Drainage (Mandatory for Most):**

**Percutaneous CT-Guided Drainage (Preferred):**
- **Success rate:** 80-90% (if unilocular, no complex septations)
- **Indications:**
  - **Unilocular abscess** (single cavity)
  - **Accessible** (CT-guided needle placement safe)
  - **No complex septations**

**Surgical Drainage (Open):**
- **Indications:**
  - **Failed percutaneous drainage** (persistent after CT-guided attempt)
  - **Multiloculated** (complex septations)
  - **Source control needed** (e.g., bowel perforation, fistula, need to remove infected hardware from spine)
  - **Large abscess** (>5-10 cm, difficult to drain percutaneously)
  - **Gas-forming organism** (extensive necrosis)

**Approach:**
- **Retroperitoneal approach** (extraperitoneal - no bowel entry)
- **Transabdominal approach** (if intra-abdominal source)

**Key Point:** **Percutaneous drainage = first-line** (80-90% success if unilocular)

---

**Antibiotics:**

**Empiric (Until cultures):**

**Primary (Suspected Hematogenous):**
- **Vancomycin** 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h + **Ceftriaxone** 2g IV q12h
- **Covers:** MRSA, MSSA, Streptococcus

**Secondary (GI Source):**
- **Vancomycin** + **Cefepime** 2g IV q8h + **Metronidazole** 500mg IV q6h
- **OR:** Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5g IV q6h
- **Covers:** MRSA, Gram-negatives, anaerobes

**Secondary (Vertebral Source):**
- **Vancomycin** + **Ceftriaxone** 2g IV q12h
- **Add rifampin + isoniazid + pyrazinamide + ethambutol** (if TB suspected - AFB pending)

**Targeted (Based on culture):**
- **MSSA:** Cefazolin 2g IV q8h OR nafcillin
- **MRSA:** Vancomycin OR daptomycin 8-10 mg/kg IV daily
- **E. coli/Enterobacteriaceae:** Ceftriaxone OR ertapenem
- **Pseudomonas:** Cefepime OR meropenem
- **Anaerobes:** Metronidazole OR carbapenems

**Duration:**
- **Primary psoas abscess:** 4-6 weeks IV
- **Secondary psoas abscess:**
  - **GI source:** 4-6 weeks IV
  - **Vertebral osteomyelitis:** 6-8 weeks IV (or longer)
  - **TB psoas abscess:** 6-12 months (RIPE therapy)

**Key Point:** **Drainage + IV antibiotics 4-6 weeks** (both mandatory)

---

## PROGNOSIS

**With Drainage + Antibiotics:**
- **Cure rate:** 80-90%
- **Mortality:** 5-10% (higher if delayed, immunocompromised)

**Without Drainage:**
- **Cure rate:** <20% (antibiotics alone fail)
- **Mortality:** 20-40%

**Factors Predicting Poor Outcome:**
1. **Delayed diagnosis** (>2-4 weeks)
2. **Immunosuppression** (diabetes, HIV, steroids)
3. **Multiloculated abscess** (complex, difficult to drain)
4. **Underlying vertebral osteomyelitis** (requires prolonged antibiotics)
5. **Polymicrobial infection** (worse than monomicrobial)
6. **Sepsis at presentation**

**Key Point:** **Drainage mandatory** (antibiotics alone = <20% cure)

---

## COMPLICATIONS

**Mnemonic: "SEPSIS-EXTEND-CHRONIC-DEATH"**

**S**epsis (bacteremia, septic shock)
**E**xtension to thigh (tracks down iliopsoas)
**P**eritonitis (if ruptures into peritoneum - rare)
**S**pinal involvement (if vertebral source)
**I**schemia (femoral vessels compressed)
**S**inus tract (chronic draining)

**E**xtension (tracks inferiorly to thigh, groin)
**X**treme complications (septic shock)
**T**hrombosis (femoral vessels - septic, compressive)
**E**ntering peritoneum (rupture → peritonitis)
**N**eurologic deficit (if spinal source → cord compression)
**D**VT (femoral vein compression, septic thrombophlebitis)

**C**hronic abscess (recurrent, inadequate drainage)
**H**ematogenous spread (endocarditis, metastatic infection)
**R**ecurrence (10-20% if inadequate drainage/antibiotics)
**O**steomyelitis (if vertebral source, may worsen)
**N**ecrosis (extensive muscle destruction)
**I**nadequate drainage (multiloculated)
**C**hronic pain (residual)

**D**eath (5-10% mortality)
**E**xtensive tissue loss
**A**bscess recurrence
**T**hromboembolism (septic emboli)
**H**emorrhage (if erodes into vessels)

**Key Point:** **Sepsis most common complication** (bacteremia, septic shock)

---

## TB PSOAS ABSCESS (Pott Disease)

**Special Features:**

**Mnemonic: "COLD-ANTERIOR-CALCIFY-LONG"**

**C**old abscess (non-tender, chronic, no acute inflammation)
**O**rigin (vertebral TB - Pott disease)
**L**umbar spine (most common TB vertebral site)
**D**rains down psoas (tracks inferiorly)

**A**nterior vertebral body (TB predilection)
**N**on-enhancing rim (on MRI - cold abscess)
**T**B specific
**E**xtraosseous soft tissue mass (large psoas mass)
**R**im calcification (chronic TB abscess)
**I**nvolves anterior vertebral body
**O**rigin vertebral osteomyelitis
**R**etroperitoneal tracking

**C**alcification (rim calcification on CT - chronic TB)
**A**bscess (large, non-tender)
**L**arge paraspinal component
**C**hronic course (weeks to months)
**I**nsidious onset
**F**atigue, weight loss, night sweats
**Y**ielding to constitutional symptoms

**L**ong treatment (6-12 months RIPE)
**O**rigin vertebral TB (Pott disease)
**N**ot acute (chronic, indolent)
**G**ibbus deformity (kyphotic angulation if severe vertebral destruction)

**Key Point:** **TB psoas = cold abscess + anterior vertebral body + rim calcification**

---

**Clinical Pearls:**
- **Psoas abscess = rim-enhancing fluid collection in psoas muscle**
- **Primary (20-30%) = hematogenous S. aureus** (immunocompromised, IVDU)
- **Secondary (70-80%) = contiguous spread** (vertebral osteomyelitis #1 cause, 40-50%)
- **Classic triad (only 30%): Hip/flank pain + fever + limp**
- **Psoas sign = pain with hip extension** (stretches inflamed psoas)
- **CT with contrast = gold standard** (90-95% sensitive)
- **Rim-enhancing hypodense collection = abscess** (CT with contrast)
- **MRI DWI bright + ADC dark = purulent abscess** (confirms diagnosis)
- **Drainage mandatory** (percutaneous CT-guided first-line, 80-90% success if unilocular)
- **Drainage + IV antibiotics 4-6 weeks** (both mandatory)
- **Antibiotics alone = <20% cure** (drainage essential)
- **Vertebral osteomyelitis = #1 secondary cause** (40-50% of secondary)
- **TB psoas = cold abscess + anterior vertebral body + rim calcification**
