## Bacterial Pneumonia - Lobar Consolidation

**Q:** What are the characteristic CT findings of lobar pneumonia including air bronchograms, and which organisms classically cause this pattern?

**A:**

## DEFINITION

**Lobar Pneumonia:** **"Homogeneous consolidation involving an entire lobe or segment with air bronchograms"**

**Pathophysiology:** Airspace filling with inflammatory exudate → complete opacification of alveoli → bronchi remain patent and air-filled (visible as air bronchograms)

**Key Point:** **Lobar consolidation + air bronchograms = bacterial pneumonia** (most commonly S. pneumoniae)

---

## CT FINDINGS

**Hallmark: Dense Homogeneous Consolidation + Air Bronchograms**

### **Mnemonic: "AIR SPACE"**

**A**ir bronchograms (branching lucencies within consolidation)
**I**nvolves entire lobe or segment (homogeneous)
**R**ight lower lobe most common (RLL > RUL > LLL)

**S**egmental or lobar distribution (respects fissures)
**P**leural effusion (parapneumonic - 40-60%)
**A**lveolar process (airspace filling)
**C**omplete opacification (dense, homogeneous)
**E**xudative (inflammatory exudate fills alveoli)

---

## AIR BRONCHOGRAMS

**Definition:** **"Branching tubular lucencies (air-filled bronchi) visible within consolidation"**

**Mechanism:**
1. **Alveoli filled** with inflammatory exudate (opaque)
2. **Bronchi remain patent** (air-filled)
3. **Contrast** between opaque alveoli and air-filled bronchi → visible bronchi

**Significance:**
- **Indicates alveolar process** (not interstitial)
- **Confirms pneumonia** (vs atelectasis, mass)
- **Suggests patent airways** (not obstructed)

**Key Point:** **Air bronchograms = alveolar filling process** (pneumonia, pulmonary edema, alveolar hemorrhage)

---

## LOBAR DISTRIBUTION

**Most Common Locations:**

1. **Right Lower Lobe (RLL)** - most common (35-40%)
2. **Right Upper Lobe (RUL)** - (20-25%)
3. **Left Lower Lobe (LLL)** - (20-25%)
4. **Left Upper Lobe (LUL)** - (10-15%)
5. **Right Middle Lobe (RML)** - (5-10%)

**Why Right Side More Common:**
- **Right main bronchus more vertical** (aspiration more likely)
- **RLL most dependent** (aspiration, secretions)

**Key Point:** **RLL most common site** for lobar pneumonia (aspiration, gravity)

---

## CLASSICAL ORGANISMS BY PATTERN

**Lobar Pneumonia (Classic):**

**Mnemonic: "PNEUMO-STREP"**

**P**neumo (***Streptococcus pneumoniae***) - **#1 cause** (70-80% of lobar pneumonias)
**N**ecrosis (Klebsiella - causes cavitation)
**E**mpyema complication (if parapneumonic effusion infected)
**U**sual bacterial cause (S. pneumoniae)
**M**ost common CAP organism
**O**ther: Legionella (lobar or patchy)

**S**taph aureus (post-influenza, necrotizing)
**T**ypical bacteria (vs atypical)
**R**isk for empyema/abscess (if necrotizing)
**E**nteric gram-negatives (Klebsiella, E. coli - aspiration)
**P**seudomonas (nosocomial, bronchiectasis)

**Key Point:** ***S. pneumoniae* = #1 cause of lobar pneumonia** (70-80%)

---

## STAGES OF LOBAR PNEUMONIA (CLASSICAL)

**Mnemonic: "CGRR" (Congestion → Red → Gray → Resolution)**

### **1. Congestion (Day 1-2):**
- **Vascular engorgement**
- **Alveolar edema**
- **Few bacteria, neutrophils**
- **Imaging:** Subtle ground-glass opacity

### **2. Red Hepatization (Day 3-4):**
- **Alveoli filled** with RBCs, neutrophils, fibrin
- **Liver-like consistency** (firm, red)
- **Imaging:** Dense consolidation

### **3. Gray Hepatization (Day 5-7):**
- **RBCs disintegrate**, fibrin predominates
- **Gray appearance** (fewer RBCs)
- **Imaging:** Continued dense consolidation

### **4. Resolution (Day 8+):**
- **Enzymatic digestion** of exudate
- **Macrophages clear debris**
- **Imaging:** Gradual clearing (2-4 weeks)

**Key Point:** **Classical stages apply to untreated pneumococcal pneumonia** (antibiotics alter progression)

---

## LOBAR PNEUMONIA vs BRONCHOPNEUMONIA

| **Feature** | **Lobar Pneumonia** | **Bronchopneumonia** |
|-------------|---------------------|----------------------|
| **Distribution** | **Lobar/segmental** (respects fissures) | **Patchy, multifocal** (peribronchial) |
| **Appearance** | **Homogeneous consolidation** | **Patchy consolidation** |
| **Air bronchograms** | **Prominent** | **Less prominent** |
| **Organism** | ***S. pneumoniae*** (typical) | **S. aureus, H. influenzae** (typical + atypical) |
| **Age** | **Adults** | **Children, elderly, immunocompromised** |
| **Clinical severity** | **Acute, severe** | **Subacute, less severe** |
| **CXR appearance** | **Lobar opacity** | **Bilateral patchy infiltrates** |

**Key Point:** **Lobar = S. pneumoniae, adult** | **Bronchopneumonia = S. aureus, Staph, viral**

---

## SILHOUETTE SIGN

**Definition:** **"Loss of normal border between consolidated lung and adjacent heart/diaphragm"**

**Mechanism:** Consolidation has same density as adjacent structure → loss of visible border

**Localizing Value:**

**Right Heart Border Lost:**
- **RML pneumonia** (right middle lobe anterior)

**Left Heart Border Lost:**
- **Lingula pneumonia** (LUL lingular segment)

**Right Hemidiaphragm Lost:**
- **RLL pneumonia** (right lower lobe)

**Left Hemidiaphragm Lost:**
- **LLL pneumonia** (left lower lobe)

**Key Point:** **Silhouette sign localizes pneumonia** (which lobe is consolidated)

---

## COMPLICATIONS

**Mnemonic: "PLEURAL ABSCESS"**

**P**leural effusion (parapneumonic - 40-60%)
**L**ung abscess (if necrotizing - Klebsiella, Staph, anaerobes)
**E**mpyema (infected parapneumonic effusion)
**U**sually resolves (2-4 weeks with treatment)
**R**espiratory failure (ARDS if severe)
**A**telectasis (mucus plugging)
**L**ymphatic spread (bacteremia)

**A**cute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS)
**B**acteremia (25-30% of pneumococcal pneumonia)
**S**eptic shock
**C**avitation (necrotizing pneumonia)
**E**mpyema necessitans (chest wall extension)
**S**epsis
**S**pread to other lobes (multilobar)

**Key Point:** **Parapneumonic effusion most common complication** (40-60% of bacterial pneumonias)

---

## TYPICAL vs ATYPICAL PNEUMONIA

**Lobar Pneumonia = "Typical" Bacterial Pneumonia:**

**Clinical Features:**
- **Acute onset** (hours to 1-2 days)
- **High fever** (>39°C), rigors
- **Productive cough** (purulent sputum)
- **Pleuritic chest pain**
- **Lobar consolidation** on CXR

**Organisms:**
- ***S. pneumoniae*** (most common)
- ***H. influenzae***
- **Klebsiella pneumoniae** (alcoholics)
- ***S. aureus*** (post-influenza)

**Key Point:** **Typical = sudden onset, high fever, lobar consolidation, S. pneumoniae**

---

**"Atypical" Pneumonia:**

**Clinical Features:**
- **Gradual onset** (days to weeks)
- **Low-grade fever**, malaise
- **Dry cough** (nonproductive)
- **Extrapulmonary symptoms** (headache, myalgias, GI symptoms)
- **Patchy or interstitial infiltrates** (not lobar)

**Organisms:**
- ***Mycoplasma pneumoniae***
- ***Chlamydia pneumoniae***
- ***Legionella pneumophila***
- **Viral** (influenza, COVID-19, RSV)

**Key Point:** **Atypical = gradual onset, dry cough, patchy infiltrates, Mycoplasma/Chlamydia/Legionella**

---

## TREATMENT

**Community-Acquired Lobar Pneumonia:**

**Outpatient (CURB-65 0-1):**
- **Amoxicillin** 1g PO TID x 5-7 days OR
- **Doxycycline** 100mg PO BID x 5-7 days (if PCN allergy) OR
- **Levofloxacin** 750mg PO daily x 5 days (if comorbidities)

**Inpatient (CURB-65 2-3):**
- **Ceftriaxone** 1-2g IV daily + **azithromycin** 500mg IV/PO daily OR
- **Beta-lactam + macrolide** combination

**ICU (CURB-65 4-5, severe):**
- **Ceftriaxone** 2g IV daily + **azithromycin** 500mg IV daily OR
- **Piperacillin-tazobactam** 4.5g IV q6h + **azithromycin** (if Pseudomonas risk)

**Key Point:** **S. pneumoniae coverage = beta-lactam** (amoxicillin, ceftriaxone)

---

## IMAGING RESOLUTION

**Timeline:**
- **Clinical improvement:** 2-3 days (fever resolution)
- **CXR improvement:** 7-10 days (begins to clear)
- **Complete CXR resolution:** 2-6 weeks (may take longer in elderly, smokers)

**Delayed Resolution Risk Factors:**
- **Age >50 years**
- **Smoking**
- **COPD, chronic lung disease**
- **Multilobar pneumonia**
- **Bacteremia**
- **Klebsiella or Legionella** (necrotizing)

**Key Point:** **CXR lags clinical improvement** (may take 2-6 weeks to fully resolve)

---

**Clinical Pearls:**
- **Lobar consolidation + air bronchograms = bacterial pneumonia** (S. pneumoniae #1)
- **RLL most common location** (right main bronchus more vertical, aspiration)
- **Air bronchograms = alveolar process** (pneumonia, edema, hemorrhage)
- **Silhouette sign localizes lobar involvement** (loss of heart/diaphragm border)
- ***S. pneumoniae* causes 70-80% of lobar pneumonias**
- **Typical pneumonia = acute, high fever, lobar consolidation, productive cough**
- **Parapneumonic effusion in 40-60%** (monitor for empyema)
- **Treatment: Beta-lactam (amoxicillin, ceftriaxone)** + macrolide (if hospitalized)
- **CXR resolution lags clinical improvement** (may take 2-6 weeks)
- **Follow-up CXR at 6 weeks** (ensure resolution, exclude underlying lesion)
