## Septic Emboli - Feeding Vessel Sign

**Q:** What is the feeding vessel sign, and what are the characteristic CT findings of septic pulmonary emboli including distribution, cavitation, and clinical sources?

**A:**

## DEFINITION

**Septic Pulmonary Emboli:** **"Infected emboli that lodge in pulmonary vessels causing infarction and abscess formation"**

**Feeding Vessel Sign:** **"Pulmonary vessel leading directly into a peripheral nodule or wedge-shaped opacity"**

**Key Point:** **Feeding vessel sign = septic emboli** (pathognomonic when present)

---

## PATHOPHYSIOLOGY

**Formation:**
1. **Source of infection** (endocarditis, thrombophlebitis, infected catheter)
2. **Infected thrombus detaches** → enters bloodstream
3. **Emboli lodge in peripheral pulmonary arteries**
4. **Vascular occlusion + bacterial seeding** → infarction + abscess
5. **CT shows vessel leading to nodule** (feeding vessel sign)

**Key Point:** **Infected emboli → peripheral infarction + abscess**

---

## CT FINDINGS

**Hallmark: Multiple Peripheral Nodules with Feeding Vessels**

### **Mnemonic: "FEEDING PERIPHERAL"**

**F**eeding vessel sign (vessel leading to nodule) - **pathognomonic**
**E**mboli (multiple, bilateral)
**E**dge of lung (peripheral, subpleural)
**D**ifferent sizes (variable nodule sizes)
**I**nfected thrombi (from endocarditis, thrombophlebitis)
**N**odules (multiple, bilateral)
**G**round-glass halo (hemorrhagic infarction)

**P**eripheral (subpleural) location
**E**ndocarditis source (#1 cause)
**R**ight side more common (tricuspid endocarditis, IVDU)
**I**nfarction (wedge-shaped opacities)
**P**leural-based nodules
**H**emorrhagic infarction (ground-glass halo)
**E**xcavation (cavitation in 30-50%)
**R**andom distribution (both lungs, all lobes)
**A**bscess formation (cavitary nodules)
**L**ower lobes (more blood flow)

**Key Point:** **Peripheral nodules + feeding vessel sign = septic emboli**

---

## FEEDING VESSEL SIGN

**Definition:** **"Pulmonary artery branch leading directly into a peripheral nodule"**

**CT Appearance:**
- **Linear opacity** (pulmonary vessel) extending from hilum → nodule
- **Connects to nodule** at one point
- **Visible on lung windows**

**Significance:**
- **80-90% specific for septic emboli**
- **Distinguishes from other causes of multiple nodules** (metastases, GPA, fungi)

**Key Point:** **Feeding vessel = pathognomonic for septic emboli**

---

## NODULE CHARACTERISTICS

**Size:** Variable (0.5-5cm)

**Number:** Multiple (usually >10)

**Distribution:**
- **Bilateral** (90-95%)
- **Random** (all lobes, not specific distribution)
- **Peripheral/subpleural** (75-80%)
- **Lower lobes > upper lobes** (increased blood flow)

**Appearance:**
- **Round or wedge-shaped**
- **Well-defined or ill-defined margins**
- **May have ground-glass halo** (hemorrhagic infarction)

**Cavitation:** 30-50% (develops 1-2 weeks after embolization)

**Key Point:** **Multiple peripheral bilateral nodules** of varying sizes

---

## CAVITATION IN SEPTIC EMBOLI

**Incidence:** 30-50% of septic emboli cavitate

**Timing:** 1-2 weeks after embolization

**Characteristics:**
- **Thin or thick-walled** (variable)
- **May have air-fluid levels**
- **Irregular inner lining**

**Significance:**
- **Confirms diagnosis** (cavitary nodules + clinical context)
- **Abscess formation** (bacterial infection + infarction)

**Key Point:** **Cavitation in 30-50%** (develops 1-2 weeks after embolization)

---

## SOURCES OF SEPTIC EMBOLI

**Mnemonic: "ENDOCARDITIS IVDU"**

**E**ndocarditis (tricuspid - IVDU, mitral/aortic - native/prosthetic) - **#1 source**
**N**ative valve endocarditis (mitral, aortic)
**D**rug use (IVDU - tricuspid endocarditis) - **most common in young adults**
**O**steomyelitis (vertebral - hematogenous spread)
**C**atheter-related (infected central line, pacemaker leads)
**A**rterial mycotic aneurysm
**R**ight-sided endocarditis (tricuspid > pulmonary valve)
**D**ental procedures (transient bacteremia)
**I**njection drug use (IVDU - S. aureus)
**T**hrombophlebitis (septic, suppurative)
**I**nfected devices (pacemaker, ICD leads)
**S**kin infections (cellulitis, abscess)

**I**VDU (intravenous drug use) - **tricuspid endocarditis**
**V**ascular access (dialysis catheter, PICC)
**D**evices (infected prosthetic valve, pacemaker)
**U**terine (septic pelvic thrombophlebitis - Lemierre's syndrome)

**Key Point:** **IVDU with tricuspid endocarditis = most common cause** in young adults

---

## ORGANISMS

**Mnemonic: "STAPH-STREP"**

**S**taph aureus (most common - 60-80%, especially IVDU)
**T**ricuspid endocarditis (IVDU)
**A**ssociated with IVDU
**P**seudomonas (IVDU, immunocompromised)
**H**ematogenous spread

**S**treptococcus (viridans group, S. bovis)
**T**hrombophlebitis (Fusobacterium - Lemierre's)
**R**ight-sided endocarditis (tricuspid)
**E**nterococci (VRE)
**P**eritoneal source (portal vein thrombophlebitis)

**Key Point:** **S. aureus = most common organism** (60-80%, especially IVDU)

---

## CLINICAL PRESENTATION

**Symptoms:**
- **Fever, chills** (bacteremia)
- **Cough** (may be nonproductive or hemoptysis)
- **Chest pain** (pleuritic - peripheral infarction)
- **Dyspnea**
- **Hemoptysis** (cavitation, infarction)

**Signs:**
- **Cardiac murmur** (endocarditis)
- **Track marks** (IVDU)
- **Peripheral stigmata** (Janeway lesions, Osler nodes, splinter hemorrhages)
- **Septic appearance** (hypotension, tachycardia)

**Labs:**
- **Positive blood cultures** (80-90%)
- **Elevated inflammatory markers** (WBC, CRP, ESR)

**Key Point:** **Fever + new murmur + pulmonary symptoms = endocarditis with septic emboli**

---

## DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS OF MULTIPLE PULMONARY NODULES

**Mnemonic: "MULTIPLE NODULES"**

**M**etastatic disease (lung/breast/renal/colon cancer)
**U**sually peripheral (like septic emboli)
**L**ower lobes (septic emboli, metastases)
**T**ube feeding (aspiration - tree-in-bud)
**I**nfection (septic emboli, fungi, TB)
**P**ulmonary emboli (bland, non-infected)
**L**ymphoma (nodular pattern)
**E**mboli (septic - feeding vessel sign)

**N**odules (multiple, bilateral)
**O**utside nodules (peripheral)
**D**rug toxicity (amiodarone, methotrexate)
**U**ncommon (sarcoidosis, silicosis)
**L**ymphangitic carcinomatosis
**E**mboli (septic, bland)
**S**arcoidosis (perilymphatic nodules)

**Key Distinguishing Feature:** **Feeding vessel sign = septic emboli**

---

## DIAGNOSIS

**Imaging:**
- **CT chest with contrast:** Multiple peripheral nodules, feeding vessel sign, cavitation
- **Echocardiography:** TTE or **TEE** (more sensitive for vegetations)

**Blood Cultures:** **3 sets** (before antibiotics)
- **Positive in 80-90%**
- **S. aureus** most common

**Duke Criteria for Endocarditis:**
- **Major:** Positive blood cultures (typical organisms), echo showing vegetation
- **Minor:** Fever, vascular phenomena (septic emboli), immunologic phenomena

**Key Point:** **Blood cultures + echo + CT chest** (confirm endocarditis with septic emboli)

---

## TREATMENT

**Antibiotics (Cover S. aureus + Strep):**

**Empiric (Until cultures available):**
- **Vancomycin** 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h (trough 15-20) + **Ceftriaxone** 2g IV daily

**If MSSA (Methicillin-Susceptible S. aureus):**
- **Nafcillin** or **oxacillin** 2g IV q4h

**If MRSA:**
- **Vancomycin** or **daptomycin** 8-10 mg/kg IV daily (daptomycin preferred if lung involvement)

**Duration:** 4-6 weeks IV (endocarditis)

**Surgery (If Indicated):**
- **Valve replacement** (large vegetation, heart failure, persistent bacteremia)
- **Rarely drain lung abscesses** (usually resolve with antibiotics)

**Key Point:** **Prolonged IV antibiotics (4-6 weeks)** + treat underlying source

---

## PROGNOSIS

**Overall Mortality:** 10-20% (endocarditis-related)

**Factors Affecting Outcome:**
- **Early diagnosis + treatment** (improved survival)
- **Source control** (valve replacement if indicated)
- **Organism** (S. aureus worse than Strep)
- **Right-sided vs left-sided endocarditis** (right-sided = better prognosis)

**Lung Findings:**
- **Cavitary nodules resolve** with antibiotics (weeks to months)
- **May leave residual scars**

**Key Point:** **Right-sided endocarditis (IVDU) = better prognosis** than left-sided

---

**Clinical Pearls:**
- **Feeding vessel sign = pulmonary vessel leading to nodule** (pathognomonic for septic emboli)
- **Multiple peripheral bilateral nodules** of varying sizes
- **Cavitation in 30-50%** (develops 1-2 weeks after embolization)
- **IVDU with tricuspid endocarditis = most common cause** in young adults
- **S. aureus = most common organism** (60-80%)
- **Fever + new murmur + pulmonary nodules = endocarditis with septic emboli**
- **Blood cultures + TEE + CT chest** (confirm diagnosis)
- **Treatment: Prolonged IV antibiotics (4-6 weeks)** + valve replacement if indicated
- **Right-sided endocarditis = better prognosis** than left-sided
- **Ground-glass halo around nodules** = hemorrhagic infarction
