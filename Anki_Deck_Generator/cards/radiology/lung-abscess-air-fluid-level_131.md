## Lung Abscess - Air-Fluid Level (Chest Radiology)

**Q:** What are the characteristic CT findings of lung abscess, including cavity characteristics, air-fluid levels, and how to distinguish it from empyema?

**A:**

## DEFINITION

**Lung Abscess:** **"Necrotic cavity within lung parenchyma containing purulent material"**

**CT Appearance:**
- **Thick-walled cavity** (>4mm, irregular)
- **Air-fluid level** (horizontal interface between air and pus)
- **Surrounding consolidation/ground-glass**
- **Makes acute angle with pleura** (intrapa renchymal origin)

**Key Point:** **Lung abscess = thick-walled cavity + air-fluid level + acute angle with chest wall**

---

## PATHOPHYSIOLOGY

**Formation Sequence:**
1. **Aspiration or hematogenous seeding** → focal pneumonia
2. **Tissue necrosis** → central liquefaction
3. **Communication with bronchus** → air enters cavity
4. **Pus accumulation** → air-fluid level forms

**Key Point:** **Air-fluid level indicates communication with airway** (drainage possible)

---

## CT FINDINGS

### **Acute Lung Abscess:**
- **Thick, irregular wall** (>4mm)
- **Shaggy inner lining** (necrotic debris)
- **Surrounding consolidation** (active inflammation)
- **Air-fluid level of equal length** in all positions
- **Dependent location** (posterior segments - aspiration)

### **Chronic Lung Abscess:**
- **Thick, well-defined wall**
- **"Star-shaped" contour** (irregular)
- **Fibrosis around cavity**
- **Less surrounding inflammation**

**Key Point:** **Acute = hazy border, thick irregular wall** | **Chronic = star-shaped, well-defined**

---

## LUNG ABSCESS vs EMPYEMA

| **Feature** | **Lung Abscess** | **Empyema** |
|-------------|------------------|-------------|
| **Location** | **Intraparenchymal** | **Pleural space** |
| **Angle with chest wall** | **Acute angle (<90°)** | **Obtuse angle (>90°)** |
| **Shape** | **Round/spherical** | **Lenticular (lens-shaped)** |
| **Wall thickness** | **Thick (>4mm), irregular** | **Thin, smooth (pleural layers)** |
| **Air-fluid level** | **Equal length all positions** | **Variable with position** |
| **Split pleura sign** | **Absent** | **Present** (enhanced pleural layers) |
| **Moves with respiration** | **No** (fixed in lung) | **Yes** (in pleural space) |

**Key Point:** **Acute angle with pleura = abscess** | **Obtuse angle = empyema**

---

## MICROBIOLOGY

**Mnemonic: "ASPIRATION"**

**A**naerobes (Peptostreptococcus, Fusobacterium, Prevotella) - **50%**
**S**taph aureus (MRSA, CA-MRSA - especially post-influenza)
**P**seudomonas aeruginosa (nosocomial, bronchiectasis)
**I**nhalation (aspiration pneumonia → abscess)
**R**ight lung more common (right main bronchus more vertical)
**A**erobic gram-negatives (Klebsiella - "currant jelly" sputum)
**T**B and fungi (chronic cavitary disease)
**I**mmunocompromised (Nocardia, fungi)
**O**ther: polymicrobial (anaerobes + aerobes)
**N**ecrotizing pneumonia (S. aureus, Klebsiella, Strep milleri group)

**Key Point:** **Anaerobes = most common** (aspiration, foul-smelling sputum)

---

## RISK FACTORS

**Mnemonic: "ABSCESS"**

**A**spiration (GERD, dysphagia, altered mental status, EtOH)
**B**ronchial obstruction (tumor, foreign body)
**S**eptic emboli (endocarditis, IVDU, thrombophlebitis)
**C**hronic lung disease (COPD, bronchiectasis, CF)
**E**sophageal disorders (achalasia, Zenker's diverticulum)
**S**teroids/immunosuppression (opportunistic pathogens)
**S**urgery/anesthesia (aspiration during intubation)

**Key Point:** **Aspiration = #1 risk factor** (posterior segments RUL, RLL, RML)

---

## CLINICAL PRESENTATION

**Classic Triad:**
1. **Subacute fever** (1-2 weeks)
2. **Productive cough** (foul-smelling, purulent)
3. **Weight loss, night sweats**

**Physical Exam:**
- **Foul-smelling sputum** (anaerobes)
- **Crackles, bronchial breath sounds**
- **Dullness to percussion**
- **Poor dentition** (aspiration source)

**Key Point:** **Foul-smelling sputum = anaerobic lung abscess**

---

## TREATMENT

**Antibiotics:**
- **Ampicillin-sulbactam** 3g IV q6h OR **piperacillin-tazobactam** 4.5g IV q6h
- **Clindamycin** 600-900mg IV q8h (if penicillin allergy)
- **Duration:** 4-6 weeks IV/PO (until cavity resolves or <2cm)

**Drainage:**
- **Postural drainage, chest physiotherapy**
- **Percutaneous drainage** if not responding (>5cm cavity)
- **Surgical resection** if refractory (rare)

**Key Point:** **Prolonged antibiotics (4-6 weeks)** until cavity resolves

---

## DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS OF CAVITARY LESIONS

**Mnemonic: "CAVITY"** (same as TB card)

**C**ancer (squamous cell most common)
**A**spergillosis, lung abscess
**V**asculitis (GPA/Wegener's)
**I**nfection (TB, Nocardia, fungi, Staph)
**T**hromboembolic (septic emboli)
**Y**outh (congenital - sequestration, cysts)

**Key Point:** **Thick wall + air-fluid level + fever = infection** (abscess vs TB)

---

**Clinical Pearls:**
- **Lung abscess = thick-walled cavity (>4mm) + air-fluid level**
- **Makes acute angle (<90°) with chest wall** (intraparenchymal)
- **Empyema makes obtuse angle (>90°)** (pleural space - lenticular shape)
- **Aspiration = #1 cause** (posterior segments RUL, RLL)
- **Anaerobes most common** (50% - foul-smelling sputum)
- **Staph aureus** post-influenza (necrotizing pneumonia)
- **Klebsiella** in alcoholics ("currant jelly" sputum)
- **Treatment: Ampicillin-sulbactam 4-6 weeks** (until cavity <2cm)
- **Chronic abscess = "star-shaped" with well-defined wall**
- **Poor dentition = aspiration risk** (anaerobic source)
