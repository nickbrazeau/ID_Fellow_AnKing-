## Histoplasmosis - Fibrosing Mediastinitis

**Q:** What is fibrosing mediastinitis, and what are the characteristic CT findings of this late complication of histoplasmosis?

**A:**

## DEFINITION

**Fibrosing Mediastinitis:** **"Progressive fibrous proliferation in the mediastinum causing compression/encasement of mediastinal structures"**

**Etiology:**
- **Histoplasmosis** (60-80% of cases) - most common infectious cause
- **Idiopathic** (15-30%)
- **Other:** TB, sarcoidosis, radiation, drugs (methysergide)

**Key Point:** **Fibrosing mediastinitis = late complication of *Histoplasma* infection** (years after initial infection)

---

## PATHOPHYSIOLOGY

**Formation Sequence:**

1. **Acute Histoplasmosis** → Mediastinal lymphadenopathy (hilar/paratracheal nodes)
2. **Caseating Necrosis** → Granuloma formation in lymph nodes
3. **Exuberant Fibrotic Response** → Extensive fibrosis beyond lymph node capsule
4. **Progressive Fibrous Proliferation** → Encasement of mediastinal structures
5. **Vascular/Airway Compression** → Symptoms (dyspnea, hemoptysis, SVC syndrome)

**Key Point:** **Exaggerated host fibrotic response** (not active infection) → progressive mediastinal fibrosis

---

## CT FINDINGS

**Hallmark: Mediastinal Soft Tissue Mass with Calcification**

### **Mnemonic: "FIBRO-HISTO"**

**F**ibrotic mass (mediastinum, infiltrative)
**I**nfiltrates vessels (SVC, pulmonary arteries/veins)
**B**ilateral (or unilateral) mediastinal involvement
**R**ight paratracheal predominance (most common location)
**O**bstruction (vascular, airway, esophageal)

**H**igh-attenuation mass (calcified lymph nodes within)
**I**rregular margins (infiltrative, not discrete)
**S**uperior vena cava compression (most common complication)
**T**hick fibrotic strands (extending into mediastinal fat)
**O**ld granulomatous infection (calcified hilar/mediastinal nodes)

---

## SPECIFIC CT FEATURES

### **1. Mediastinal Mass:**

**Characteristics:**
- **Soft tissue density mass** (infiltrative, not discrete)
- **Right paratracheal predominance** (60-70% of cases)
- **Irregular margins** (infiltrates mediastinal fat)
- **Calcification** (punctate, diffuse, or dense)
- **May extend into hila** (bilateral or unilateral)

**Key Point:** **Infiltrative mediastinal mass with calcification = fibrosing mediastinitis**

---

### **2. Calcification Pattern:**

**Types:**
- **Punctate calcifications** (scattered throughout mass)
- **Diffuse calcification** (extensive)
- **Dense calcified lymph nodes** (within fibrous mass)
- **"Eggshell" calcification** (peripheral rim - less common)

**Key Point:** **Calcification within mediastinal mass + right paratracheal location = histoplasmosis**

---

### **3. Vascular Involvement (Most Important):**

**Superior Vena Cava (SVC) - Most Common:**
- **Compression** or **obstruction** (70-80% of symptomatic cases)
- **Narrowing** (focal or diffuse)
- **Collateral vessel formation** (azygos, intercostal veins)
- **Symptoms:** Facial/upper extremity edema, dyspnea, headache (SVC syndrome)

**Pulmonary Arteries:**
- **Stenosis or occlusion** (unilateral or bilateral)
- **Pulmonary hypertension** (if bilateral involvement)
- **Perfusion defects** on V/Q scan

**Pulmonary Veins:**
- **Stenosis or occlusion** (most concerning)
- **Pulmonary edema** (if bilateral)
- **Hemoptysis** (pulmonary venous hypertension)

**Key Point:** **Pulmonary vein stenosis = worst complication** (pulmonary edema, hemoptysis, high mortality)

---

### **4. Airway Involvement:**

**Bronchial Compression:**
- **Narrowing of main bronchi** (right > left)
- **Lobar atelectasis** (if complete obstruction)
- **Post-obstructive pneumonia**
- **Symptoms:** Cough, dyspnea, recurrent pneumonia

**Tracheal Compression:**
- **Less common** (trachea more rigid)
- **Extrinsic compression** (may see mass effect)

**Key Point:** **Bronchial stenosis → recurrent pneumonia** (post-obstructive)

---

### **5. Esophageal Involvement:**

**Characteristics:**
- **Extrinsic compression** (narrowing)
- **Dysphagia** (progressive)
- **Less common** than vascular involvement (10-20%)

**Key Point:** **Dysphagia + mediastinal mass = consider fibrosing mediastinitis**

---

## CLASSIFICATION

**Two Patterns:**

### **1. Localized (Focal) Fibrosing Mediastinitis:**

**Characteristics:**
- **Discrete mass** (well-defined)
- **Single mediastinal compartment** (usually right paratracheal)
- **May be resectable** (surgical option)
- **Better prognosis**

### **2. Diffuse (Infiltrative) Fibrosing Mediastinitis:**

**Characteristics:**
- **Infiltrative process** (ill-defined margins)
- **Multiple mediastinal compartments**
- **Not resectable** (too extensive)
- **Worse prognosis** (progressive compression)

**Key Point:** **Diffuse pattern = worse prognosis** (progressive, not resectable)

---

## CLINICAL PRESENTATION

**Mnemonic: "SVC SYMPTOMS"**

**S**VC syndrome (facial edema, plethora, dilated neck veins)
**V**ascular compression (SVC, pulmonary arteries/veins)
**C**ough, dyspnea (airway compression)

**S**tenosis of pulmonary vessels (hemoptysis, pulmonary edema)
**Y**ears after initial infection (chronic, progressive)
**M**ediastinal mass (incidental finding or symptomatic)
**P**ulmonary hypertension (if bilateral PA stenosis)
**T**hrombosis (SVC, due to compression)
**O**bstruction (vascular > airway > esophageal)
**M**ortality high if pulmonary vein involvement
**S**upine dyspnea (orthopnea if SVC syndrome)

**Key Point:** **SVC syndrome = most common presentation** (facial/upper extremity edema)

---

## DIAGNOSIS

**CT Chest with Contrast:**
- **Essential for diagnosis**
- **Defines extent of fibrosis**
- **Identifies vascular/airway compression**
- **Assesses calcification pattern**

**Additional Imaging:**
- **MRI chest:** Better soft tissue characterization, vascular assessment
- **V/Q scan or CTA:** Pulmonary artery/vein patency
- **Pulmonary angiography:** If surgical intervention considered

**Serologic Testing:**
- ***Histoplasma* antibody:** May be positive (suggests prior infection)
- ***Histoplasma* antigen:** Usually negative (not active infection)
- **Skin testing:** Not helpful (historical infection common in endemic areas)

**Biopsy:**
- **Rarely performed** (risk of bleeding, difficult access)
- **Histology:** Dense fibrosis, calcified granulomas, **no organisms** (not active infection)

**Key Point:** **CT chest with contrast = diagnostic** (mediastinal fibrosis + calcification + vascular compression)

---

## DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS

**Mediastinal Masses with Calcification:**

**Mnemonic: "CALCIFIED MEDIASTINUM"**

**C**ancer (thymoma, teratoma with calcification)
**A**neurysm (calcified aortic aneurysm)
**L**ymphoma (treated - post-radiation fibrosis)
**C**ongenital cysts (bronchogenic, pericardial)
**I**nfection (TB - calcified lymph nodes)
**F**ibrosing mediastinitis (**histoplasmosis - most common**)
**I**diopathic mediastinal fibrosis
**E**sophageal duplication cyst
**D**ermoid/teratoma

**M**etastatic disease (treated - calcified nodes)
**E**nlargedlymph nodes (sarcoidosis with calcification)
**D**rugs (methysergide - retroperitoneal/mediastinal fibrosis)
**I**atrogenic (post-radiation, post-surgical)
**A**ortic atherosclerosis (calcified)
**S**ilicosis (calcified hilar nodes - "eggshell")
**T**B mediastinal lymphadenitis (caseating, calcified)
**I**nfection sequelae (histoplasmosis, TB)
**N**eoplasm (treated lymphoma)
**U**ncommon (IgG4-related disease)
**M**alignancy (treated, post-chemo/radiation)

**Key Point:** **Endemic area + calcified mediastinal mass + SVC syndrome = histoplasmosis-related fibrosing mediastinitis**

---

## TREATMENT

**No Effective Medical Therapy:**

- **Antifungal therapy NOT effective** (not active infection, exuberant fibrosis)
- **Corticosteroids NOT effective** (controversial, some case reports of benefit)
- **Observation:** For asymptomatic or mildly symptomatic patients

**Interventional Options:**

**Endovascular Stenting:**
- **SVC stenting** (for SVC syndrome - palliative)
- **Pulmonary artery stenting** (for PA stenosis)
- **High restenosis rate** (fibrosis progresses through stent)

**Surgical Options:**
- **Surgical resection** (if localized, resectable mass)
- **Bypass grafts** (SVC bypass, PA bypass)
- **High morbidity/mortality** (difficult surgery, extensive fibrosis)

**Lung Transplantation:**
- **Reserved for bilateral pulmonary vein stenosis** (life-threatening)
- **High-risk procedure**

**Key Point:** **No effective medical therapy** | **Endovascular stenting = palliative for SVC syndrome**

---

## PROGNOSIS

**Variable:**

**Localized Fibrosing Mediastinitis:**
- **Stable or slowly progressive**
- **Better prognosis** (may remain asymptomatic)

**Diffuse Fibrosing Mediastinitis:**
- **Progressive**
- **Worse prognosis** (progressive vascular/airway compromise)

**Mortality:**
- **Pulmonary vein stenosis = highest mortality** (30-40% 5-year mortality)
- **SVC syndrome alone = better prognosis** (stenting palliative)
- **Bilateral PA stenosis → pulmonary hypertension → poor prognosis**

**Key Point:** **Pulmonary vein stenosis = worst prognosis** (pulmonary edema, hemoptysis, high mortality)

---

**Clinical Pearls:**
- **Fibrosing mediastinitis = late complication of histoplasmosis** (years after initial infection)
- **Exuberant fibrotic response** (not active infection) → progressive mediastinal fibrosis
- **CT: Infiltrative mediastinal mass + calcification + right paratracheal location**
- **SVC compression = most common complication** (SVC syndrome: facial edema, dilated neck veins)
- **Pulmonary vein stenosis = worst complication** (pulmonary edema, hemoptysis, high mortality)
- **No effective medical therapy** (antifungals, steroids not helpful)
- **Endovascular stenting = palliative** (SVC stenting for SVC syndrome)
- **Endemic area (Ohio/Mississippi River Valley) + mediastinal mass = consider histoplasmosis**
- **Diffuse pattern = worse prognosis** (progressive, not resectable)
- **Biopsy rarely needed** (CT findings + endemic exposure sufficient)
