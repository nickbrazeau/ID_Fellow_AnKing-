## Osteomyelitis - Periosteal Reaction

**Q:** What are the characteristic X-ray and MRI findings of osteomyelitis including periosteal reaction patterns, and how does acute differ from chronic osteomyelitis on imaging?

**A:**

## DEFINITION

**Osteomyelitis:** **"Infection/inflammation of bone and bone marrow"**

**X-ray Hallmark:** **Periosteal reaction + bone destruction + soft tissue swelling**

**MRI Hallmark:** **Bone marrow edema (T2 hyperintense) + enhancement + cortical destruction**

**Key Point:** **Periosteal reaction = bone's response to infection** (infection lifting periosteum)

---

## PATHOPHYSIOLOGY

**Routes of Infection:**

**Mnemonic: "HEMATOGENOUS-CONTIGUOUS-DIRECT"**

**Hematogenous (Most Common in Children):**
- **Bacteremia** seeding metaphysis (sluggish blood flow in sinusoidal vessels)
- **Children:** Metaphysis (rich blood supply)
- **Adults:** Vertebral bodies (Batson plexus)
- **S. aureus** most common organism

**Contiguous Spread (Most Common in Adults):**
- **Diabetic foot infection** (most common adult source)
- **Pressure ulcers** (sacrum, heels)
- **Post-operative** (orthopedic hardware)
- **Adjacent soft tissue infection**

**Direct Inoculation:**
- **Trauma** (open fracture)
- **Surgery** (orthopedic procedures)
- **Penetrating injury** (gunshot, nail)

**Key Point:** **Hematogenous = children | Contiguous = adults (diabetic foot)**

---

## X-RAY FINDINGS

**Hallmark: Periosteal Reaction + Bone Destruction**

### **Mnemonic: "PERIOSTEAL-DESTRUCTION"**

**P**eriosteal reaction (interrupted, lamellated, spiculated)
**E**arly findings (soft tissue swelling first)
**R**eaction types (single-layer, multi-layer, Codman triangle)
**I**nterrupted (aggressive pattern - infection/tumor)
**O**steolysis (bone destruction, lucency)
**S**oft tissue swelling (early finding, <24 hours)
**T**ime delay (10-14 days for X-ray changes)
**E**ndosteal scalloping (inner cortex erosion)
**A**ggressive appearance (moth-eaten, permeative)
**L**ucency (bone destruction, lytic lesions)

**D**estruction patterns (geographic, moth-eaten, permeative)
**E**levated periosteum (separated from cortex by pus)
**S**equestrum (dead bone fragment - chronic)
**T**ime to visible changes (10-14 days minimum)
**R**eactive sclerosis (chronic osteomyelitis)
**U**niform cortical thickening (chronic)
**C**ortical breach (erosion through cortex)
**T**ransition zone (poorly defined - aggressive)
**I**nvolucrum (new bone formation around sequestrum - chronic)
**O**steolytic lesions (lucent areas)
**N**ew bone formation (periosteal, chronic)

**Key Point:** **X-ray changes lag 10-14 days** (MRI detects earlier, within 1-3 days)

---

## PERIOSTEAL REACTION PATTERNS

**Periosteal Reaction = Periosteum Lifting from Cortex**

### **Mnemonic: "SINGLE-MULTI-CODMAN-SUNBURST"**

**S**ingle layer (solid, uninterrupted - benign, healing fracture)
**I**nterrupted (aggressive - infection, tumor)
**N**ew bone (periosteum laying down bone)
**G**radual (smooth, continuous)
**L**amellated (onion-skin - Ewing sarcoma, chronic infection)
**E**levated (separated from cortex)

**M**ulti-layered (lamellated, onion-skin)
**U**ninterrupted (benign) vs interrupted (aggressive)
**L**ifting of periosteum (by pus, tumor, hemorrhage)
**T**ype determines aggressiveness
**I**nfection causes interrupted pattern

**C**odman triangle (periosteum lifted at one end - aggressive)
**O**steomyelitis (causes aggressive patterns)
**D**isplaced periosteum (lifted by pus)
**M**alignancy (also causes Codman triangle)
**A**ggressive lesion (tumor, infection)
**N**on-continuous (interrupted)

**S**unburst (radiating spicules - aggressive)
**U**sually osteosarcoma (but can be infection)
**N**ew bone perpendicular to cortex
**B**urst pattern (radiating)
**U**ncommon in infection (more tumor)
**R**adiating spicules
**S**piky appearance
**T**umor more common (but infectious can mimic)

**Key Point:** **Interrupted periosteal reaction = aggressive** (infection or tumor)

---

## X-RAY PATTERNS OF BONE DESTRUCTION

**Mnemonic: "GEOGRAPHIC-MOTH-EATEN-PERMEATIVE"**

**Geographic (Type I - Slow Growing, Benign):**
- **Well-defined borders** (sclerotic rim)
- **Punched-out appearance**
- **Slowly progressive infection** (chronic osteomyelitis)
- **Brodie abscess** (subacute osteomyelitis)

**Moth-Eaten (Type II - Moderately Aggressive):**
- **Multiple small lytic areas** (2-5mm)
- **Poorly defined margins**
- **Acute osteomyelitis** (bacterial)
- **Looks like moth-eaten cloth**

**Permeative (Type III - Highly Aggressive):**
- **Infiltrative pattern** (multiple tiny holes <1mm)
- **Very poorly defined**
- **Acute aggressive infection** (can't distinguish from tumor)
- **Worst prognosis pattern**

**Key Point:** **Moth-eaten = acute osteomyelitis** (most common pattern)

---

## ACUTE vs CHRONIC OSTEOMYELITIS (X-RAY)

**Acute Osteomyelitis (0-2 weeks):**
- **Soft tissue swelling** (earliest finding, within 24-48 hours)
- **Periosteal reaction** (after 7-10 days)
- **Bone destruction** (moth-eaten pattern, after 10-14 days)
- **No sequestrum** (acute)
- **No involucrum** (acute)

**Chronic Osteomyelitis (>4-6 weeks):**
- **Sequestrum** (dead bone fragment - **hallmark**, dense/sclerotic)
- **Involucrum** (new bone shell around sequestrum)
- **Cortical thickening** (sclerosis, reactive)
- **Cloaca** (sinus tract through involucrum draining pus)
- **Sclerotic bone** (dense, white on X-ray)

**Key Point:** **Sequestrum + involucrum = chronic osteomyelitis**

---

## MRI FINDINGS

**MRI = Gold Standard (90-95% Sensitive, 80-90% Specific)**

**Hallmark: Bone Marrow Edema + Enhancement**

### **Mnemonic: "T1-T2-ENHANCE-DWI"**

**T1 Weighted:**
- **Hypointense marrow signal** (replaces normal fatty marrow - dark)
- **Normal marrow = bright** (fat), **infected marrow = dark** (edema, pus)
- **Cortical disruption** (breach in cortex)

**T2/STIR:**
- **Hyperintense marrow signal** (edema, inflammation - bright)
- **Soft tissue edema** (periosteal, adjacent muscle)
- **Sinus tracts** (hyperintense tracks to skin)
- **Abscess formation** (rim-enhancing fluid collection)

**Post-Contrast T1 Fat-Saturated:**
- **Enhancement** of infected marrow (inflamed tissue enhances)
- **Rim-enhancing abscess** (if abscess formed)
- **Periosteal enhancement**
- **Soft tissue phlegmon/abscess** enhancement

**DWI/ADC:**
- **DWI hyperintense** (restricted diffusion if purulent)
- **Confirms abscess** (vs phlegmon)

**Key Point:** **T1 hypointense + T2 hyperintense marrow = osteomyelitis**

---

## MRI ACUTE vs CHRONIC

**Acute Osteomyelitis:**
- **Bone marrow edema** (T1 dark, T2 bright)
- **Enhancement** (diffuse marrow enhancement)
- **Soft tissue edema** (periosteal, adjacent)
- **May have abscess** (rim-enhancing)

**Chronic Osteomyelitis:**
- **Sequestrum** (T1/T2 dark - no signal, dead bone)
- **Involucrum** (surrounding new bone)
- **Cloaca/sinus tracts** (T2 bright tracks)
- **Sclerotic bone** (low signal T1/T2)
- **Less edema** (compared to acute)

**Key Point:** **Sequestrum on MRI = T1/T2 hypointense** (no signal - dead bone)

---

## TIMING OF IMAGING CHANGES

**X-Ray:**
- **Soft tissue swelling:** 24-48 hours
- **Periosteal reaction:** 7-10 days
- **Bone destruction:** 10-14 days (30-50% bone loss before visible)
- **Sequestrum:** >4-6 weeks

**MRI:**
- **Bone marrow edema:** 1-3 days (earliest)
- **Soft tissue changes:** 1-3 days
- **Abscess formation:** 5-14 days

**CT:**
- **Cortical destruction:** 7-10 days
- **Sequestrum:** Best modality for detecting sequestrum

**Key Point:** **MRI detects osteomyelitis 10-14 days before X-ray**

---

## CLINICAL PRESENTATION

**Acute Osteomyelitis:**
- **Fever** (50-70%)
- **Bone pain** (90-100%, localized, constant)
- **Swelling** (over affected bone)
- **Refusal to bear weight** (children)
- **Warmth, erythema** (overlying skin)

**Chronic Osteomyelitis:**
- **Draining sinus** (60-80%)
- **Bone pain** (intermittent)
- **Fever** (uncommon)
- **Recurrent flares**

**Key Point:** **Bone pain + fever + local swelling = osteomyelitis**

---

## ORGANISMS

**Mnemonic: "STAPH-SALMONELLA-PSEUDO-TB"**

**Hematogenous (Any Age):**
- ***Staphylococcus aureus*** (80-90% - **most common overall**)
- **MRSA** (increasing, especially USA300)

**Neonates (<1 month):**
- ***S. aureus***
- **Group B Streptococcus** (GBS)
- **Gram-negatives** (E. coli, Klebsiella)

**Children:**
- ***S. aureus*** (80%)
- ***Streptococcus pyogenes*** (Group A Strep)
- **Kingella kingae** (6 months - 4 years)

**Adults:**
- ***S. aureus*** (80-90%)
- **Coagulase-negative staph** (prosthetic joint)

**Sickle Cell Disease:**
- ***Salmonella*** species (30-50%)
- ***S. aureus***

**Diabetic Foot:**
- **Polymicrobial** (Staph, Strep, Gram-negatives, anaerobes)

**IV Drug Users:**
- ***S. aureus*** (MRSA)
- **Pseudomonas aeruginosa** (axial skeleton, vertebrae)
- ***Candida*** species

**Vertebral Osteomyelitis:**
- ***S. aureus***
- ***Mycobacterium tuberculosis*** (Pott disease)
- **Brucella** (endemic areas)

**Puncture Wound Through Shoe:**
- ***Pseudomonas aeruginosa***

**Key Point:** **S. aureus = #1 cause overall** | **Salmonella = sickle cell**

---

## DIAGNOSIS

**Imaging:**
- **X-ray** (initial, but insensitive early)
- **MRI with contrast** (gold standard, 90-95% sensitive)
- **CT** (best for sequestrum detection)
- **Nuclear medicine** (if MRI contraindicated):
  - **Bone scan** (Tc-99m MDP - sensitive but not specific)
  - **WBC scan** (In-111 or Tc-99m HMPAO - more specific)

**Labs:**
- **ESR/CRP elevated** (90-95% - ESR >70 mm/hr suggestive)
- **WBC elevated** (50-60% only)
- **Blood cultures** (positive 30-60%)

**Tissue Diagnosis:**
- **Bone biopsy** (gold standard for organism identification)
- **Culture + histopathology**
- **Before antibiotics** if possible (ideally)

**Key Point:** **Bone biopsy = gold standard** for microbiologic diagnosis

---

## TREATMENT

**Medical:**

**Empiric Antibiotics (Until cultures):**
- **Vancomycin** 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h + **Ceftriaxone** 2g IV q12h OR **Cefepime** 2g IV q8h
- **Covers:** MRSA, MSSA, Streptococcus, Gram-negatives

**Targeted (Based on culture):**
- **MSSA:** Cefazolin 2g IV q8h OR nafcillin
- **MRSA:** Vancomycin OR daptomycin 8-10 mg/kg IV daily
- **Pseudomonas:** Cefepime OR meropenem + aminoglycoside (consider)
- **Salmonella (sickle cell):** Ceftriaxone OR ciprofloxacin

**Duration:**
- **Acute osteomyelitis:** 4-6 weeks IV (minimum)
- **Chronic osteomyelitis:** 6-12 weeks (or longer)
- **Vertebral osteomyelitis:** 6-8 weeks minimum

**Oral Step-Down:**
- **After clinical improvement** (afebrile, declining CRP)
- **High bioavailability agents** (fluoroquinolones, linezolid, TMP-SMX)
- **NOT for severe cases** (extensive bone destruction, bacteremia)

**Key Point:** **4-6 weeks IV antibiotics minimum** for acute osteomyelitis

---

**Surgical Indications:**

**Mnemonic: "DEBRIDE-CHRONIC-SEQUESTRUM"**

**D**ebridement (remove dead/infected bone)
**E**xtensive bone destruction
**B**one abscess (Brodie abscess drainage)
**R**efractory to antibiotics (persistent infection)
**I**nadequate source control (medical failure)
**D**raining sinus tract
**E**mpirical failure (antibiotics not working)

**C**hronic osteomyelitis (almost always requires surgery)
**H**ardware-related (remove infected prosthesis/hardware)
**R**ecurrent infection (relapse after medical treatment)
**O**steonecrotic bone (sequestrum removal)
**N**on-union fracture (infected)
**I**nfected joint (adjacent septic arthritis)
**C**omplications (abscess, fistula)

**S**equestrum (must be removed surgically - cannot be eradicated with antibiotics alone)
**E**xtensive dead bone
**Q**uiescent infection (reactivates without removal)
**U**nresolved despite medical therapy
**E**xcise necrotic tissue
**S**urgical debridement
**T**issue non-viable
**R**emove infected hardware
**U**nresponsive to antibiotics alone
**M**ultiple surgeries may be needed

**Key Point:** **Sequestrum must be removed** (antibiotics cannot penetrate dead bone)

---

## PROGNOSIS

**Acute Osteomyelitis:**
- **Cure rate:** 80-90% (with appropriate antibiotics + surgery if needed)
- **Recurrence:** 10-20%

**Chronic Osteomyelitis:**
- **Cure rate:** 50-70% (requires surgery + prolonged antibiotics)
- **Recurrence:** 20-30%
- **Risk factors for failure:**
  - **Inadequate debridement**
  - **Retained sequestrum**
  - **Inadequate antibiotic duration**
  - **Peripheral vascular disease** (diabetic foot)

**Key Point:** **Chronic osteomyelitis requires surgery** (medical therapy alone fails)

---

## COMPLICATIONS

**Mnemonic: "CHRONIC-FRACTURE-MALIGNANCY"**

**C**hronic osteomyelitis (if untreated or inadequate treatment)
**H**ematogenous spread (sepsis, endocarditis)
**R**ecurrence (10-30%)
**O**steonecrosis (bone death, sequestrum)
**N**on-union fracture (if pathologic fracture)
**I**nadequate growth (children - physeal damage)
**C**ontracture (joint stiffness, ankylosis)

**F**racture (pathologic fracture through weakened bone)
**R**efractory infection (antibiotic-resistant)
**A**bscess formation (Brodie abscess, soft tissue)
**C**hronic draining sinus
**T**issue destruction (extensive)
**U**ncontrolled infection (sepsis)
**R**esidual deformity (limb length discrepancy, angular deformity)
**E**pilobar deformity (growth plate damage)

**M**alignancy (squamous cell carcinoma in chronic sinus tract - rare, 0.2-1.5%)
**A**myloidosis (chronic inflammation - rare)
**L**imb loss (amputation if refractory diabetic foot osteomyelitis)
**I**nfection spread (septic arthritis, soft tissue abscess)
**G**rowth disturbance (children)
**N**ecrosis (extensive bone death)
**A**bscess (intraosseous, soft tissue)
**N**eed for amputation (diabetic foot, refractory)
**C**hronic pain
**Y**ielding to sepsis

**Key Point:** **Chronic sinus tracts can develop squamous cell carcinoma** (rare, after decades)

---

## SPECIAL SCENARIOS

**Diabetic Foot Osteomyelitis:**
- **Probe-to-bone test** (positive if metal probe contacts bone - 90% PPV)
- **Polymicrobial** (Staph, Strep, Gram-negatives, anaerobes)
- **Often requires surgical debridement** + prolonged antibiotics
- **MRI** to assess extent (soft tissue, bone involvement)

**Vertebral Osteomyelitis (Pott Disease if TB):**
- **Back pain + fever + elevated ESR**
- **MRI:** Vertebral body + disc involvement (disc space narrowing, endplate destruction)
- **Biopsy** (CT-guided) for culture
- **Epidural abscess** complication (cord compression - surgical emergency)

**Puncture Wound Through Shoe:**
- **Pseudomonas aeruginosa** (#1 cause)
- **Foot bones** (metatarsals)
- **Ciprofloxacin** (good bone penetration)

**Key Point:** **Diabetic foot: Probe-to-bone positive = 90% PPV for osteomyelitis**

---

**Clinical Pearls:**
- **Periosteal reaction + bone destruction = osteomyelitis**
- **X-ray changes lag 10-14 days** (30-50% bone loss required)
- **MRI = gold standard** (90-95% sensitive, detects within 1-3 days)
- **Interrupted periosteal reaction = aggressive** (infection or tumor)
- **Moth-eaten pattern = acute osteomyelitis**
- **Sequestrum + involucrum = chronic osteomyelitis**
- **S. aureus = #1 cause overall** (80-90%)
- **Salmonella = sickle cell disease**
- **Pseudomonas = puncture wound through shoe**
- **Bone biopsy = gold standard** for microbiologic diagnosis
- **4-6 weeks IV antibiotics minimum** (acute)
- **Chronic osteomyelitis requires surgery** (debridement + antibiotics)
- **Sequestrum must be removed surgically** (antibiotics cannot penetrate)
- **Probe-to-bone positive = 90% PPV** for diabetic foot osteomyelitis
