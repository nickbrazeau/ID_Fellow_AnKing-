## Perinephric Abscess - Gerota Fascia

**Q:** What are the characteristic CT findings of perinephric abscess including its relationship to Gerota fascia, and how do you distinguish from renal abscess and pararenal abscess?

**A:**

## DEFINITION

**Perinephric Abscess:** **"Collection of pus in perinephric space (between renal capsule and Gerota fascia)"**

**CT Hallmark:** **Rim-enhancing fluid collection in perinephric space, confined by Gerota fascia**

**Key Point:** **Perinephric abscess = confined by Gerota fascia** (vs pararenal = outside Gerota)

---

## ANATOMY

**Retroperitoneal Spaces:**

**Mnemonic: "THREE-SPACES-GEROTA-LATEROCONAL"**

1. **Anterior Pararenal Space:**
   - **Between:** Peritoneum (anterior) and anterior renal fascia (posterior)
   - **Contents:** Pancreas, duodenum, ascending/descending colon

2. **Perirenal (Perinephric) Space:**
   - **Between:** Renal capsule (inner) and Gerota fascia (outer)
   - **Contents:** Kidney, adrenal gland, proximal ureter, perinephric fat
   - **Enclosed by:** Gerota fascia (anterior renal fascia + posterior renal fascia + lateroconal fascia)
   - **Communicates:** Both perirenal spaces communicate inferiorly (no barrier at pelvic brim)

3. **Posterior Pararenal Space:**
   - **Between:** Posterior renal fascia (anterior) and transversalis fascia (posterior)
   - **Contents:** Fat, lymphatics

**Gerota Fascia:**
- **Encases:** Kidney + adrenal + perinephric fat
- **Composed of:** Anterior renal fascia + posterior renal fascia (fuse superiorly, laterally)
- **Opens inferiorly:** No barrier at pelvic brim (allows inferior spread)

**Key Point:** **Perinephric space = between renal capsule and Gerota fascia** (enclosed space)

---

## PATHOPHYSIOLOGY

**Routes of Infection:**

**Mnemonic: "RENAL-PARENCHYMA-EXTENSION-HEMATOGENOUS-TRAUMA"**

**From Renal Parenchyma (70-80% - Most Common):**
- **Renal abscess rupture** (intraparenchymal abscess ruptures through capsule into perinephric space)
- **Pyelonephritis extension** (infection extends beyond capsule)
- **Urinary obstruction** (obstructing stone → pyelonephritis → perinephric extension)

**Extension from Adjacent Organs (<10%):**
- **Pancreatic abscess** (acute pancreatitis)
- **Bowel perforation** (diverticulitis, appendicitis - rare, usually anterior pararenal)
- **Psoas abscess** (tracks superiorly)

**Hematogenous (<5%):**
- **Bacteremia** (direct seeding - rare)
- ***S. aureus*** (most common hematogenous organism)

**Trauma (<5%):**
- **Penetrating trauma** (gunshot, stab)
- **Blunt trauma** (renal laceration → hematoma → superinfection)

**Key Point:** **Renal abscess rupture = #1 cause** (70-80%, ruptures through capsule into perinephric space)

---

## CT FINDINGS (Gold Standard)

**CT = Preferred Modality**

**Hallmark: Rim-Enhancing Fluid Collection in Perinephric Space**

---

### **CT WITH CONTRAST FINDINGS:**

**Mnemonic: "RIM-ENHANCE-GEROTA-THICKENED-KIDNEY"**

**Abscess:**
- **Hypodense fluid collection** (10-30 HU - pus, necrosis)
- **Rim enhancement** (abscess wall - vascularized granulation tissue)
- **Central non-enhancing** (purulent fluid)
- **Loculated** (septations within abscess)
- **Gas bubbles** (if gas-forming organism - 10-20%)
- **Confined by Gerota fascia** (does not extend to anterior pararenal or posterior pararenal)

**Additional Findings:**
- **Perinephric fat stranding** (edema, inflammation - 90-95%)
- **Kidney displaced or compressed** (mass effect from abscess)
- **Underlying renal pathology:**
  - **Renal abscess** (intraparenchymal abscess that ruptured)
  - **Pyelonephritis** (striated nephrogram)
  - **Obstructing stone** (hydronephrosis)
- **Inferior extension** (tracking down to pelvis - Gerota fascia opens inferiorly)
- **Adjacent organ involvement** (if source from pancreas, bowel)

**Key Point:** **Rim-enhancing fluid collection + thickened enhancing Gerota fascia = perinephric abscess**

---

### **UNENHANCED CT:**

- **Hypodense collection** (fluid attenuation)
- **Thickened Gerota fascia** (may be visible)
- **Gas bubbles** (if present)
- **Cannot distinguish from hematoma** without contrast

**Key Point:** **Contrast essential** to confirm abscess (rim enhancement)

---

## MRI FINDINGS

**MRI Characteristics:**

**T1:**
- **Hypointense fluid collection** (pus - low signal)
- **Rim:** Variable signal
- **Thickened Gerota fascia** (low signal)

**T2:**
- **Hyperintense fluid collection** (very bright - pus, edema)
- **Rim:** Hypointense (fibrous capsule)
- **Perinephric fat edema** (hyperintense)

**Post-contrast T1 Fat-Saturated:**
- **Rim enhancement** (abscess wall)
- **Gerota fascia enhancement** (thickened enhancing fascia)
- **Central non-enhancing** (pus)

**DWI/ADC:**
- **DWI hyperintense** (restricted diffusion - purulent abscess)
- **ADC hypointense** (low ADC values)
- **Confirms purulent nature**

**Key Point:** **MRI less commonly used** (CT faster, more available for acute abdomen)

---

## RENAL vs PERINEPHRIC vs PARARENAL ABSCESS

**Anatomic Location:**

| **Feature** | **Renal Abscess** | **Perinephric Abscess** | **Pararenal Abscess** |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------------------|-----------------------|
| **Location** | **Within kidney parenchyma** | **Between capsule and Gerota fascia** | **Outside Gerota fascia** (anterior or posterior pararenal space) |
| **Relationship to kidney** | **Intraparenchymal** | **Extrinsic, displaces kidney** | **Separate from kidney** |
| **Gerota fascia** | **Inside** (within kidney) | **Confined by Gerota** | **Outside Gerota** |
| **Origin** | Pyelonephritis, hematogenous | Renal abscess rupture, pyelonephritis extension | Bowel (diverticulitis), pancreas, psoas |
| **Renal displacement** | **No** (abscess within kidney) | **Yes** (mass effect) | **Yes** (may displace kidney) |

**Key Point:** **Renal = within kidney** | **Perinephric = between capsule and Gerota** | **Pararenal = outside Gerota**

---

## CLINICAL PRESENTATION

**Symptoms:**
- **Fever** (90-100%)
- **Flank pain** (80-90%)
- **Chills** (60-70%)
- **Nausea/vomiting** (40-50%)
- **Dysuria** (30-40% - if concurrent UTI)

**Physical Exam:**
- **Flank tenderness** (90%)
- **Palpable flank mass** (30-40%)
- **Costovertebral angle tenderness** (CVAT - 80-90%)

**Presentation:**
- **Subacute/chronic** (weeks, insidious onset)
- **May be less acute** than pyelonephritis (walled-off infection)

**Key Point:** **Fever + flank pain + flank mass = perinephric abscess** (subacute presentation)

---

## LABS

**Inflammatory Markers:**
- **WBC elevated** (leukocytosis 80-90%)
- **CRP/ESR elevated** (90-95%)

**Urinalysis:**
- **Pyuria** (70-80% - if communication with collecting system)
- **Bacteriuria** (60-70%)
- **May be NORMAL** (30% - if no communication with collecting system)

**Urine Culture:**
- **Positive 60-70%** (if communication with collecting system)
- **May be negative** (30% - if walled-off, no drainage into collecting system)

**Blood Cultures:**
- **Positive 40-50%**

**Key Point:** **Urine may be normal** (30% - if abscess walled-off, no communication with collecting system)

---

## ORGANISMS

**Mnemonic: "E-COLI-PROTEUS-KLEBSIELLA-STAPH"**

**From Renal Parenchyma (Ascending UTI):**
- ***E. coli*** (40-60% - most common)
- ***Proteus mirabilis*** (10-20%)
- ***Klebsiella pneumoniae*** (10-15%)
- **Enterococcus** (5-10%)
- **Polymicrobial** (20-30%)

**Hematogenous:**
- ***Staphylococcus aureus*** (most common hematogenous - 10-20%)

**Tuberculosis:**
- ***Mycobacterium tuberculosis*** (5-10% - endemic areas, immunocompromised)
- **"Cold abscess"** (non-enhancing rim, chronic)

**Key Point:** **E. coli = most common** (40-60%) | **S. aureus = hematogenous**

---

## DIAGNOSIS

**Imaging:**
- **CT with contrast** (gold standard - 90-95% sensitive)
- **MRI with DWI** (if CT contraindicated)
- **Ultrasound** (bedside, but limited by gas/ribs)

**Drainage with Culture:**
- **CT-guided aspiration** or **surgical drainage**
- **Gram stain + culture (aerobic, anaerobic, AFB, fungal)**

**Key Point:** **CT with contrast + drainage with culture** (diagnostic and therapeutic)

---

## TREATMENT

**Drainage (Mandatory):**

**Percutaneous CT-Guided Drainage (Preferred):**
- **Success rate:** 80-90%
- **Indications:**
  - **Unilocular or minimally septated**
  - **Accessible** (safe CT-guided approach)

**Surgical Drainage (Open or Laparoscopic):**
- **Indications:**
  - **Failed percutaneous drainage** (persistent after CT-guided attempt)
  - **Multiloculated** (complex septations)
  - **Source control needed** (e.g., obstructing stone removal, nephrectomy if non-functioning kidney)
  - **Large abscess** (>10 cm)
  - **Gas-forming organism** (extensive necrosis)

**Nephrectomy:**
- **Indications:**
  - **Non-functioning kidney** (complete destruction)
  - **Failed drainage** (persistent infection despite drainage + antibiotics)
  - **Extensive necrosis**

**Key Point:** **Drainage mandatory** (percutaneous first-line, 80-90% success)

---

**Antibiotics:**

**Empiric (Until cultures):**
- **Ceftriaxone** 2g IV q12h + **Metronidazole** 500mg IV q6h
- **OR:** Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5g IV q6h
- **Covers:** Gram-negatives, anaerobes, Enterococcus

**Add Vancomycin** if:
- **MRSA risk** (IVDU, catheter, healthcare-associated)
- **S. aureus suspected** (hematogenous source)

**Targeted (Based on culture):**
- **E. coli/Enterobacteriaceae:** Ceftriaxone OR ertapenem
- **Proteus:** Ceftriaxone OR ciprofloxacin
- **Klebsiella:** Ceftriaxone (if susceptible) OR carbapenem (if ESBL)
- **S. aureus (MSSA):** Cefazolin or nafcillin
- **S. aureus (MRSA):** Vancomycin or daptomycin
- **TB:** RIPE therapy (rifampin, isoniazid, pyrazinamide, ethambutol) x 6-12 months

**Duration:**
- **4-6 weeks IV** (after drainage)
- **Longer if:** Complex abscess, delayed drainage, immunocompromised

**Key Point:** **Drainage + IV antibiotics 4-6 weeks** (both mandatory)

---

## PROGNOSIS

**With Drainage + Antibiotics:**
- **Cure rate:** 85-90%
- **Mortality:** 5-10%

**Without Drainage:**
- **Cure rate:** <20% (antibiotics alone fail)
- **Mortality:** 30-50%

**Factors Predicting Poor Outcome:**
1. **Delayed diagnosis** (>2-4 weeks)
2. **Diabetes mellitus** (worse prognosis)
3. **Immunosuppression** (HIV, transplant, neutropenia)
4. **Multiloculated abscess** (difficult to drain)
5. **Bilateral** (both kidneys involved)
6. **Non-functioning kidney** (may require nephrectomy)

**Key Point:** **Drainage mandatory** (antibiotics alone = <20% cure)

---

## COMPLICATIONS

**Mnemonic: "RUPTURE-SEPSIS-SPREAD-CHRONIC"**

**R**upture (into peritoneum - peritonitis, 5-10%)
**U**ncontrolled sepsis (bacteremia, septic shock)
**P**soas abscess (inferior extension along psoas muscle)
**T**hrombosis (renal vein thrombosis - rare)
**U**ntreated (30-50% mortality without drainage)
**R**ecurrence (10-20% if inadequate drainage)
**E**xtension (to adjacent organs - pancreas, bowel, pleura)

**S**epsis (bacteremia, septic shock - 30-40%)
**E**xtension (inferior to pelvis, lateral to pararenal space)
**P**eritonitis (if ruptures into peritoneum)
**S**ubphrenic abscess (superior extension)
**I**nadequate drainage (multiloculated)
**S**inus tract (chronic draining - rare)

**S**pread to pleura (empyema if superior extension)
**P**leural effusion (reactive, sympathetic - 20-30%)
**R**enal failure (if bilateral or single kidney)
**E**xtensive destruction (non-functioning kidney)
**A**djacent organ involvement
**D**eath (5-10% mortality despite treatment)

**C**hronic abscess (recurrent, inadequate treatment)
**H**ematogenous spread (metastatic infection)
**R**ecurrence (10-20%)
**O**bstruction (if stone not removed)
**N**ephrectomy (may be required if non-functioning)
**I**nadequate response (persistent infection)
**C**hronic pain

**Key Point:** **Sepsis (30-40%) most common complication** | **Inferior extension → psoas abscess**

---

## DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS

**Other Perinephric Fluid Collections:**

**Mnemonic: "ABSCESS-HEMATOMA-URINOMA-LYMPHOCELE"**

**Abscess:**
- **Rim enhancement** + restricted diffusion + fever + leukocytosis

**Hematoma (Post-Trauma):**
- **History of trauma**
- **High T1 signal** (blood products if subacute)
- **No enhancement** (vs abscess enhances)
- **No restricted diffusion**

**Urinoma (Urine Leak):**
- **History of trauma, surgery, obstruction**
- **Fluid attenuation** (same as urine, ~0-10 HU)
- **No enhancement** (no rim)
- **Delayed CT:** Opacifies with contrast (urine accumulation)

**Lymphocele (Post-Transplant, Post-Surgery):**
- **History of renal transplant or retroperitoneal surgery**
- **Simple fluid** (serous, ~0-10 HU)
- **No enhancement**
- **No restricted diffusion**

**Pancreatic Pseudocyst:**
- **History of pancreatitis**
- **Anterior pararenal space** (vs perinephric space)
- **Amylase/lipase elevated**

**Key Point:** **Rim enhancement + restricted diffusion + fever = abscess** (vs hematoma/urinoma/lymphocele)

---

**Clinical Pearls:**
- **Perinephric abscess = pus between renal capsule and Gerota fascia**
- **Rim-enhancing fluid collection + thickened enhancing Gerota fascia = perinephric abscess**
- **Renal abscess rupture = #1 cause** (70-80%, ruptures through capsule into perinephric space)
- **Fever + flank pain + flank mass = perinephric abscess** (subacute presentation)
- **Urine may be normal (30%)** - if abscess walled-off, no communication with collecting system
- **E. coli = most common** (40-60%) | **S. aureus = hematogenous**
- **CT with contrast = gold standard** (90-95% sensitive)
- **Drainage mandatory** (percutaneous first-line, 80-90% success)
- **Drainage + IV antibiotics 4-6 weeks** (both mandatory)
- **Antibiotics alone = <20% cure** (drainage essential)
- **Renal = within kidney | Perinephric = between capsule and Gerota | Pararenal = outside Gerota**
- **Inferior extension → psoas abscess** (Gerota fascia opens inferiorly)
