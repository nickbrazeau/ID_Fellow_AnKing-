## Acute Bronchitis: Etiology and Antibiotic Indications

**Q:** What is the most common etiology of acute bronchitis in healthy adults, and when are antibiotics indicated?

**A:**

**Etiology:**
- **Viral (90-95%)**: Rhinovirus, enterovirus, influenza A/B, parainfluenza, coronavirus, RSV, human metapneumovirus
- **Bacterial (1-10%)**: *Mycoplasma pneumoniae* (<1%), *Chlamydia pneumoniae*, *Bordetella pertussis* (~10% of cough >2 weeks)

**Clinical Features:**
- **Acute cough** (primary symptom) lasting <3 weeks
- ± Sputum production (purulent sputum does NOT indicate bacterial infection)
- ± Wheezing, dyspnea
- **Normal chest X-ray** (vs. pneumonia)

**Antibiotic Indications (very limited):**

| Indication | Antibiotic | Rationale |
|------------|-----------|-----------|
| **Suspected pertussis** | Azithromycin 500 mg × 1, then 250 mg daily × 4 days | ↓ Transmission (does NOT shorten symptoms) |
| **Confirmed pertussis outbreak** | Macrolide (azithromycin/clarithromycin) | Epidemiologic control |
| **Age ≥65 + pneumonia risk** | Consider if high suspicion bacterial | Prevent progression to pneumonia |

**NOT Indicated:**
- Routine use in healthy adults (guideline recommendation: **do NOT use antibiotics**)
- Purulent sputum alone
- *M. pneumoniae* or *C. pneumoniae* (self-limited)

**Pearl:** **ACCP guidelines:** Antibiotics NOT recommended for acute bronchitis unless pertussis confirmed/suspected.
