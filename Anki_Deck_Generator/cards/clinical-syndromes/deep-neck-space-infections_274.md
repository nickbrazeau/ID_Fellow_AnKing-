## Deep Neck Space Infections: Ludwig Angina and Abscesses

**Q:** What are the key clinical features and management priorities for major deep neck space infections?

**A:**

| Infection | Etiology | Clinical Features | Complications | Imaging |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|---------------|---------|
| **Ludwig Angina** | Dental (90%): mandibular molars (#2, #3) | **Bilateral submandibular + sublingual space** cellulitis → tongue elevation/posterior displacement → **airway obstruction** | Descending mediastinitis (71% via retropharyngeal), aspiration | **Contrast CT neck** |
| **Peritonsillar Abscess** | Polymicrobial: *S. pyogenes*, oral anaerobes | **Unilateral** tonsillar swelling, uvular deviation, trismus, "hot potato voice," drooling | Carotid sheath involvement, mediastinitis | Clinical ± CT if failed drainage |
| **Retropharyngeal Abscess** | Children: suppurative lymphadenitis post-URI; Adults: trauma, foreign body | Fever, dysphagia, neck stiffness, **prevertebral soft tissue widening** on lateral neck X-ray | **Descending necrotizing mediastinitis**, sepsis | **Contrast CT neck/chest** |

**Microbiology (all odontogenic infections):**
- **Polymicrobial**: Viridans streptococci, *S. anginosus* group, oral anaerobes (*Prevotella, Fusobacterium*, anaerobic cocci)
- 63% anaerobes, 37% aerobes

**Management Priorities:**
1. **Airway assessment FIRST** (Ludwig angina requires **early intubation/tracheostomy**)
2. IV antibiotics: **Ampicillin-sulbactam 3 g q6h** OR **clindamycin 900 mg q8h** (covers anaerobes)
3. **Surgical drainage** (I&D) + source control (dental extraction if odontogenic)
4. Contrast-enhanced CT neck (after airway secured)

**Pearl:** Ludwig angina = **board-like induration** of submandibular space; do NOT attempt I&D (risk worsening airway).
