## Splenic Abscess: Etiology, Microbiology, and Treatment

**Q:** What are the major causes of splenic abscess, and how is it managed?

**A:**

**Etiology (hematogenous spread):**
- **Infective endocarditis** (~20% of cases) - septic emboli → splenic infarction (5.4%) → abscess (5% of infarcts)
- **Bacteremia from other sources**: UTI, osteomyelitis, typhoid, malaria, otitis
- **Trauma** (direct inoculation)
- **Immunocompromised states**: Diabetes, malignancy, immunosuppression

**Microbiology:**

| Patient Population | Common Organisms |
|--------------------|------------------|
| **General** | Gram-positive cocci: *Streptococcus viridans, S. aureus* (most common) |
| **Enteric source** | *E. coli, Salmonella*, enterococcus, anaerobes |
| **Immunocompromised** | Fungi (*Candida, Aspergillus*), mycobacteria, *Actinomyces* |
| **Endocarditis-associated** | Organism matches blood culture |

**Clinical Features:**
- LUQ pain, fever, splenomegaly (classic triad - variable)
- Often nonspecific: malaise, nausea, left shoulder pain (Kehr sign)

**Diagnosis:**
- **CT abdomen with contrast** (imaging of choice)
- Blood cultures (often positive)

**Treatment:**

1. **Antimicrobials** (median duration: **45 days**)
   - **Empiric**: Vancomycin + piperacillin-tazobactam (covers MRSA + enteric GNR + anaerobes)
   - **Tailor** to blood/operative cultures

2. **Source control:**
   - **Percutaneous drainage** (if accessible, unilocular)
   - **Splenectomy** (if drainage fails, immunocompromised, or endocarditis requiring valve surgery)

**Pearl:** Splenic abscess in setting of endocarditis may require **simultaneous valve replacement + splenectomy**.
