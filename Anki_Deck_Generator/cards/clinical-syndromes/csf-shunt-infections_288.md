## CSF Shunt Infections: Microbiology and Management

**Q:** What are the most common pathogens causing CSF shunt infections, and what is the optimal management strategy?

**A:**

**Microbiology:**
- **Coagulase-negative staphylococci** (esp. *S. epidermidis*) - **most common (~40%)**
- ***S. aureus*** (~20%)
- **Gram-negative bacilli** (14%): *E. coli, Pseudomonas, Klebsiella*
- ***Cutibacterium acnes*** (formerly *Propionibacterium*) - **indolent infections**, increasingly recognized
- Biofilm formation common

**Diagnosis (HCRN Consensus Criteria):**
- Positive CSF/wound swab/pseudocyst fluid culture or Gram stain
- Shunt erosion (visible hardware)
- Abdominal pseudocyst (VP shunt)
- Bacteremia (ventriculoatrial shunt)

**Management:**

**Surgical Approach (Optimal - >95% success):**
1. **Remove infected shunt**
2. Place **external ventricular drain (EVD)**
3. IV antibiotics until CSF sterile (serial cultures negative)
4. **Replace new shunt** after CSF clearance

**Alternative (lower success):**
- Externalization with replacement
- Immediate replacement (not recommended)
- Antibiotics alone (rarely successful due to biofilm)

**Antibiotic Regimen:**

**Empiric:**
- **Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h** (Gram-positive, including MRSA)
- \+ **Ceftazidime 2 g IV q8h** OR **cefepime 2 g IV q8h** OR **meropenem 2 g IV q8h** (Gram-negative/Pseudomonas)

**Duration (after shunt removal):**
- **Gram-positives**: 10-14 days
- **Gram-negatives/fungi**: 14-21 days

**Pearl:** Shunt removal + EVD + antibiotics = gold standard (>95% cure); antibiotics alone rarely successful due to biofilm.
