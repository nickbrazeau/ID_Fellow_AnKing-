## Mediastinitis: Etiology and Management

**Q:** What are the main types of mediastinitis, their etiologies, and management approaches?

**A:**

**Types of Mediastinitis:**

| Type | Etiology | Microbiology | Mortality |
|------|----------|--------------|-----------|
| **Post-sternotomy** | Intraoperative contamination, wound infection seeding mediastinum | *S. aureus* (including MRSA), CoNS (60-80%) | Variable |
| **Descending necrotizing mediastinitis (DNM)** | Deep neck infections (Ludwig angina, peritonsillar abscess, dental infections) spreading to mediastinum | Polymicrobial: oral flora (streptococci, *Prevotella, Fusobacterium*, anaerobes) | High (up to 40%) |
| **Fibrosing mediastinitis** | Granulomatous (histoplasmosis), autoimmune | Non-infectious | Chronic |

**Descending Necrotizing Mediastinitis:**
- Affects **2-5% of deep neck infections**
- Spreads via fascial planes (retropharyngeal, prevertebral, carotid sheath)
- **High mortality** (~40%) if delayed treatment

**Management:**

**Post-Sternotomy Mediastinitis:**
1. **Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics**:
   - **Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h** (cover MRSA)
   - \+ **Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 g IV q6h** OR carbapenem
2. **Surgical debridement + drainage**
3. Tailor antibiotics to culture results

**Descending Necrotizing Mediastinitis:**
1. **Early surgical intervention** (nearly all cases require surgery)
2. **Broad-spectrum antibiotics** (cover oral flora + anaerobes):
   - **Ampicillin-sulbactam 3 g IV q6h** OR
   - **Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 g IV q6h** OR
   - **Carbapenem + vancomycin** (if severe)
3. Aggressive debridement, drainage, source control
