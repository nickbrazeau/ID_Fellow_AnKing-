## Myocarditis and Pericarditis: Etiology and Treatment

**Q:** What are the major etiologies of myocarditis and pericarditis, and what is the evidence-based treatment approach?

**A:**

**MYOCARDITIS:**

**Etiology (Developed Countries):**
- **Viral (most common)**: Parvovirus B19, HHV-6, Coxsackievirus, enterovirus, EBV, CMV, adenovirus, COVID-19, influenza
- **Bacterial** (rare unless immunocompromised)
- **Autoimmune**: SLE, Wegener granulomatosis, giant cell arteritis

**Clinical Presentation:**
- Fatigue, chest pain, CHF, arrhythmias, cardiac arrest
- 50% develop dilated cardiomyopathy
- 1-year mortality: 20% (56% at 4 years)

**Treatment:**
- **Supportive care** + GDMT for heart failure
- Antibiotics if bacterial (rare)
- Immunosuppression (steroids + azathioprine/cyclosporine) for eosinophilic, giant cell, or cardiac sarcoidosis

---

**PERICARDITIS:**

**Etiology:**
- **Idiopathic/viral** (80-90% in developed countries)
- **Tuberculous** (most common in developing countries)
- Bacterial (purulent), autoimmune, uremia, malignancy

**Risk of Constriction by Etiology:**
- Low (1%): Idiopathic/viral
- Intermediate (2-5%): Autoimmune, neoplastic
- High (20-30%): Bacterial (TB, purulent)

**Treatment:**

| Agent | Dose | Duration | Evidence |
|-------|------|----------|----------|
| **NSAIDs** | Ibuprofen 600-800 mg TID OR aspirin 750-1000 mg TID | 7-14 days | Relief in 85-90% |
| **Colchicine** | 0.6 mg BID (if >70 kg) OR 0.6 mg daily | **3 months** (first episode) | **↓ recurrence by 50%** |
| **Corticosteroids** | Low-dose prednisone | Varies | Only if contraindication to NSAIDs/colchicine OR autoimmune |

**Pearl:** Colchicine recommended for **all** first episodes of pericarditis; steroids NOT first-line (↑ recurrence risk).
