## Question

How is epiglottitis managed? Use **"Airway Emergency (Do NOT Examine Throat), Secure Airway in OR → Ceftriaxone × 7-10d"** framework.

## Answer

### **Definition:**

**Epiglottitis (Supraglottitis):**
- **Life-threatening inflammation** of epiglottis and supraglottic structures
- **Airway emergency** (risk of complete obstruction)

### **Epidemiology:**

**Pre-Hib Vaccine:**
- **Children 2-7 years** (most common)
- ***Haemophilus influenzae* type b (Hib)** - 90% of cases

**Post-Hib Vaccine (Current):**
- **Adults > children** (rare in vaccinated children)
- **Broader microbiology** (not just Hib)

### **Clinical Presentation:**

**Classic "4 Ds":**
1. **Dysphagia** (difficulty swallowing)
2. **Drooling** (unable to swallow secretions)
3. **Dysphonia** (muffled "hot potato" voice)
4. **Distress** (respiratory distress, tripod positioning)

**Additional Features:**
- **Acute onset** (hours)
- **High fever** (>39°C)
- **Sore throat**
- **Stridor** (late finding, indicates impending obstruction)
- **Tripod position** (sitting upright, leaning forward, neck extended)
- **Toxic appearance**

**Key:** **Absence of cough** (distinguishes from croup)

### **Diagnosis:**

**IMPORTANT: Do NOT Examine Throat or Lay Patient Supine**
- **Risk of precipitating complete airway obstruction**
- **Do NOT use tongue depressor**

**Lateral Neck X-Ray (If Stable):**
- **"Thumb sign"** (swollen epiglottis resembles thumb)
- **Thickened aryepiglottic folds**
- **Do NOT delay definitive airway** for imaging if unstable

**Direct Visualization:**
- **ONLY in controlled setting** (operating room with anesthesia, ENT, equipment for emergency airway)
- **"Cherry-red" epiglottis**

**Blood Cultures:**
- **Positive in 10-30%** (bacteremia common)

### **Microbiology:**

**Adults (Current Era):**
- ***Streptococcus pneumoniae*, *Streptococcus pyogenes*** (most common)
- ***Haemophilus influenzae* (non-typeable)**
- ***Staphylococcus aureus***
- **Culture-negative** (50%)

**Children (Unvaccinated or Vaccine Failure):**
- ***Haemophilus influenzae* type b (Hib)**

### **Treatment:**

**1. AIRWAY MANAGEMENT (PRIORITY):**

**Do NOT Agitate Patient:**
- **Keep upright** (tripod position)
- **Minimize anxiety** (crying worsens obstruction)
- **Do NOT lay supine**
- **Supplemental oxygen** (if tolerated)

**Secure Airway:**
- **Intubation in OR** (controlled setting with anesthesia, ENT, equipment)
- **Awake fiberoptic intubation** (adults, if cooperative)
- **Emergency cricothyrotomy/tracheostomy** (if cannot intubate)

**Indications for Intubation:**
- **Children:** Nearly all (lower threshold)
- **Adults:** Stridor, respiratory distress, unable to swallow secretions, hypoxia

**Extubation:**
- **After 24-48h** (once inflammation/edema resolved, air leak around tube)

**2. Antibiotics (Start After Airway Secured):**

**Empiric:**
- **Ceftriaxone 1-2g IV daily × 7-10 days** (adults)
- **Ceftriaxone 50-100 mg/kg/day IV daily × 7-10 days** (children)

**Alternative:**
- **Cefotaxime 2g IV q8h**
- **If PCN allergy:** Levofloxacin 500mg IV daily (adults) OR azithromycin (children)

**Pathogen-Directed:**
- **Narrow based on blood culture** (if positive)

**Duration:** 7-10 days (IV initially, oral step-down once extubated/improving)

**3. Adjunctive Therapy:**

**Corticosteroids (Controversial):**
- **Dexamethasone 0.5 mg/kg IV × 1** (may reduce edema, duration of intubation)
- **Evidence limited** (not routinely recommended, but often given)

**4. Supportive Care:**
- **IV fluids** (NPO initially)
- **ICU monitoring**

### **Differential Diagnosis:**

**Croup (Laryngotracheobronchitis):**
- **Gradual onset, barky cough, stridor**
- **Subglottic narrowing** ("steeple sign" on X-ray)
- **Less toxic appearing**

**Peritonsillar Abscess:**
- **Trismus, uvular deviation**
- **More gradual onset**

**Retropharyngeal Abscess:**
- **Prevertebral soft tissue swelling** on lateral neck X-ray

**Angioedema:**
- **Lip/tongue swelling, urticaria**
- **Allergic/medication-related**

### **Complications:**

**Acute:**
- **Complete airway obstruction** (mortality 10-20% if untreated)
- **Aspiration**
- **Pneumonia**

**Rare:**
- **Epiglottic abscess**
- **Mediastinitis** (if deep space infection)

### **Prognosis:**

**With Appropriate Management:**
- **Excellent prognosis** (mortality <1%)
- **Most extubated within 24-48h**

**Without Treatment:**
- **High mortality** (10-20%, airway obstruction)

### **Prevention:**

**Hib Vaccine:**
- **Routine childhood vaccination** (2, 4, 6, 12-15 months)
- **Dramatically reduced incidence** (>99% decrease in children)

## Key Points

### **Airway Emergency:**
- **Do NOT examine throat, lay supine, agitate patient**
- **Risk of precipitating complete obstruction**

### **Secure Airway in OR:**
- **Controlled intubation** (anesthesia, ENT, equipment ready)
- **Children:** Nearly all intubated
- **Adults:** If stridor, respiratory distress, cannot swallow

### **4 Ds:**
- **Dysphagia, Drooling, Dysphonia, Distress**
- **+ Tripod positioning**

### **"Thumb Sign" on Lateral Neck X-Ray:**
- **Swollen epiglottis**
- **Do NOT delay airway management** for imaging if unstable

### **Ceftriaxone × 7-10 Days:**
- **After airway secured**
- **Covers *S. pneumoniae*, *H. influenzae*, *S. pyogenes***

### **Clinical Pearls:**
- **Airway emergency:** Do NOT examine throat, secure airway in OR
- **4 Ds:** Dysphagia, drooling, dysphonia, distress (+ tripod position)
- **"Thumb sign"** on lateral neck X-ray (swollen epiglottis)
- **Intubate in OR** (controlled setting), extubate after 24-48h
- **Ceftriaxone × 7-10 days** (after airway secured)
- **Hib vaccine:** Dramatically reduced pediatric cases (>99%)
