## Subdural Empyema and Spinal Epidural Abscess

**Q:** What are the etiologies and management of spinal epidural abscess (SEA) and subdural empyema?

**A:**

**SPINAL EPIDURAL ABSCESS (SEA):**

**Definition:** Suppurative infection between spinal dura and vertebral periosteum → **neurosurgical emergency**

**Etiology (Source of Infection):**
- **Hematogenous spread** (25-50%): bacteremia, endocarditis
- **Contiguous spread**: vertebral osteomyelitis, discitis, paraspinal abscess, psoas abscess, decubitus ulcers
- **Direct inoculation** (15%): neurosurgery, lumbar puncture, epidural analgesia

**Microbiology:**
- ***S. aureus*** (including MRSA) - **~65% of cases**
- Gram-negatives: *E. coli* (UTI source), *P. aeruginosa* (IVDU)
- Streptococci, anaerobes (polymicrobial if contiguous)

**Clinical Features:**
- **Classic triad** (only 10-15%): fever, back pain, neurologic deficits
- Progression: radiculopathy → motor weakness → **paralysis** (hours to days)

**Diagnosis:**
- **MRI with gadolinium** (>90% sensitivity) - imaging of choice
- Blood cultures (often positive)

**Treatment:**
1. **Urgent neurosurgical consult** - decompressive laminectomy + debridement
2. **Empiric antibiotics** (before/concurrent with surgery):
   - **Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h** + **ceftriaxone 2 g IV q12h** OR **cefepime 2 g IV q8h**
   - Add **metronidazole** if contiguous/polymicrobial source
3. Duration: **4-8 weeks IV** (adjust based on source control, clinical response)

**Pearl:** Any delay in surgery ↑ risk of permanent neurologic deficits; suspect in IVDU, diabetics, immunosuppressed with back pain + fever.
