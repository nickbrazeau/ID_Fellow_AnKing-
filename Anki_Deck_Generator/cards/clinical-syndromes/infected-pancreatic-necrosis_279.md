## Infected Pancreatic Necrosis: Diagnosis and Management

**Q:** When should infected pancreatic necrosis be suspected, and what is the approach to diagnosis and antibiotic therapy?

**A:**

**Clinical Suspicion (peaks week 2-4 after AP onset):**
- Failure to improve or **clinical deterioration after 7-10 days** of hospitalization
- Persistent SIRS, fever, ↑ WBC despite supportive care
- Pancreatic/extrapancreatic necrosis on imaging

**Diagnosis:**

| Modality | Finding | Sensitivity |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| **Contrast-enhanced CT** (after 72h) | **Gas in necrotic collection** (highly specific) | Only 12-22% have gas |
| **FNA (CT-guided)** | Gram stain + culture | **NOT routinely recommended** (only if failed empiric antibiotics) |
| **Clinical + imaging** | Preferred approach | High clinical utility |

**Antibiotic Therapy:**

**Indications:**
- Suspected/confirmed infected necrosis
- Start empirically when clinically suspected

**Preferred Agents (good pancreatic penetration):**
- **Carbapenems** (meropenem 1 g IV q8h, imipenem 500 mg IV q6h)
- **Fluoroquinolones** (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin) + **metronidazole**
- **3rd/4th-gen cephalosporins** + metronidazole

**Microbiology:**
- Gram-negatives (*E. coli, Klebsiella, Pseudomonas*)
- Anaerobes
- Enterococcus

**Other Key Interventions:**
- **Early enteral nutrition** (↓ risk of infection)
- **Delayed intervention** (postpone drainage/debridement ≥4 weeks if stable)
- **Step-up approach**: antibiotics → percutaneous drainage → minimally invasive → open necrosectomy

**Pearl:** Prophylactic antibiotics in sterile necrosis NOT recommended (no mortality benefit).
