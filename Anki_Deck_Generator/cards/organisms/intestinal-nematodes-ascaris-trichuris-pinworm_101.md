## Intestinal Nematodes: Ascaris, Trichuris (Whipworm), Pinworm

**Q:** What are the clinical features, complications, and treatment of Ascaris lumbricoides, Trichuris trichiura (whipworm), and Enterobius vermicularis (pinworm)?

**A:**

## OVERVIEW

| **Parasite** | **Transmission** | **Key Clinical Feature** | **Diagnosis** | **Treatment** |
|--------------|------------------|--------------------------|---------------|---------------|
| ***Ascaris lumbricoides*** | Fecal-oral (eggs in soil) | **Intestinal obstruction**, **Löffler syndrome** | Stool O&P (eggs) | Albendazole OR Mebendazole (single dose) |
| ***Trichuris trichiura*** (Whipworm) | Fecal-oral (eggs in soil) | **Chronic dysentery**, **rectal prolapse** (heavy infection) | Stool O&P (barrel-shaped eggs) | **Mebendazole** 3 days (more effective than albendazole) |
| ***Enterobius vermicularis*** (Pinworm) | Fecal-oral, autoinfection | **Perianal itching** (nocturnal) | **Tape test** (perianal) | Albendazole OR Mebendazole (single dose, repeat in 2 weeks) |

**Key Point:** **Ascaris = largest intestinal nematode**, **Trichuris = whipworm**, **Pinworm = perianal itching**

---

## 1. ASCARIS LUMBRICOIDES (ASCARIASIS)

**Organism:** *Ascaris lumbricoides* (**largest intestinal nematode** - adults 15-35 cm long)

**Transmission:**
- **Fecal-oral** (ingestion of embryonated eggs in contaminated soil/food)

**Epidemiology:**
- **~800 million infected** worldwide
- **Most common helminth infection** globally
- **Endemic:** Tropical/subtropical regions (sub-Saharan Africa, Asia, Latin America)

---

### **Life Cycle:**

1. **Eggs ingested** → Hatch in small intestine → **Larvae**
2. **Larvae penetrate intestinal wall** → Bloodstream → **Lungs** (7-10 days)
3. **Ascend trachea** → Swallowed → **Small intestine**
4. **Adult worms** in jejunum → Lay eggs (200,000/day) → Excreted in stool
5. **Eggs embryonate** in soil (2-3 weeks) → Infectious

**Key Point:** **Larval migration through lungs** (pulmonary phase)

---

### **Clinical Features:**

---

#### **A. Pulmonary Phase (Larval Migration - "Löffler Syndrome"):**

**Timing:** 7-10 days after ingestion

**Clinical Features:**
- **Dry cough, dyspnea, wheezing**
- **Fever, substernal chest discomfort**
- **Transient infiltrates** on CXR
- **Eosinophilia** (marked)

**Key Point:** **Löffler syndrome** = transient pulmonary infiltrates + eosinophilia (larval migration)

---

#### **B. Intestinal Phase:**

**Light Infection (Asymptomatic - Most Common):**
- **No symptoms**

**Heavy Infection (>50-100 worms):**
- **Vague abdominal discomfort, distension**
- **Malnutrition** (compete for nutrients)
- **Failure to thrive** (children)

---

#### **C. Complications (Heavy Infection):**

---

##### **1. Intestinal Obstruction (Most Common Serious Complication):**

**Mechanism:**
- **Large worm bolus** blocks intestinal lumen (typically ileum)

**Risk Factors:**
- **Heavy infection** (>50-100 worms)
- **Children** (narrow intestinal lumen)
- **Anthelmintic treatment** (can stimulate worm migration)

**Clinical Features:**
- **Colicky abdominal pain, distension**
- **Nausea, vomiting**
- **Obstipation** (inability to pass stool/gas)
- **Palpable mass** (worm bolus)

**Imaging:**
- **X-ray/CT:** Dilated bowel loops, worm bolus (tubular filling defects)

**Management:**
- **Conservative:** Nasogastric suction, IV fluids, **anthelmintic therapy** (if partial obstruction)
- **Surgery:** If complete obstruction, peritonitis, or no response to conservative management

**Key Point:** **Intestinal obstruction = most common serious complication** (especially children)

---

##### **2. Biliary/Pancreatic Ascariasis:**

**Mechanism:**
- **Adult worms migrate** into biliary tree or pancreatic duct

**Clinical Features:**
- **Biliary colic** (RUQ pain)
- **Cholangitis, cholecystitis**
- **Pancreatitis**
- **Hepatic abscess** (rare)

**Diagnosis:**
- **Ultrasound/ERCP:** Worms in biliary tree ("spaghetti sign")

**Management:**
- **ERCP:** Extract worms + anthelmintic therapy

---

##### **3. Other Complications (Rare):**
- **Appendicitis** (worms in appendix)
- **Intestinal perforation**
- **Volvulus**

---

### **Diagnosis:**

| **Test** | **Specimen** | **Sensitivity** | **Notes** |
|----------|--------------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Stool O&P (microscopy)** | Stool | **>90%** (if adult worms present) | **Oval, brown, thick-walled eggs** (fertilized) |
| **Worms passed** | Stool/vomitus | **100%** | Adult worms visible (large, pink/white) |
| **Imaging** | X-ray/CT/Ultrasound | Variable | Worms in intestine/biliary tree (tubular structures) |

**Key Point:** **Stool O&P = see eggs** (or adult worms passed in stool/vomitus)

---

### **Treatment:**

**First-Line:**

| **Agent** | **Dosing** | **Duration** |
|-----------|------------|--------------|
| **Albendazole** (preferred) | **400 mg PO x 1 dose** | **Single dose** |
| **OR Mebendazole** | **100 mg PO BID** OR **500 mg PO x 1 dose** | **3 days** OR **Single dose** |
| **OR Ivermectin** | **150-200 μg/kg PO x 1 dose** | **Single dose** |

**Important:**
- **Avoid paralyzing agents** (pyrantel pamoate, ivermectin) if **intestinal obstruction** (may worsen obstruction)
- **Use albendazole or mebendazole** for obstruction

**Key Point:** **Albendazole = single dose** (high cure rate)

---

### **Prevention:**

- **Improved sanitation** (prevent fecal contamination of soil)
- **Wash produce**
- **Mass drug administration** (MDA) in endemic areas

---

## 2. TRICHURIS TRICHIURA (TRICHURIASIS / WHIPWORM)

**Organism:** *Trichuris trichiura* ("whipworm" - anterior end thin like whip handle)

**Transmission:**
- **Fecal-oral** (ingestion of embryonated eggs in contaminated soil/food)

**Epidemiology:**
- **~500 million infected** worldwide
- **Endemic:** Tropical/subtropical regions

---

### **Life Cycle:**

1. **Eggs ingested** → Hatch in small intestine → **Larvae**
2. **Larvae mature** in small intestine → **Adults** in cecum/colon
3. **Adults burrow** anterior end into colonic mucosa (cause bleeding) → Lay eggs
4. **Eggs excreted** in stool → Embryonate in soil (2-3 weeks)

**Key Point:** **Adults burrow into colonic mucosa** (anterior "whip" end)

---

### **Clinical Features:**

**Light Infection (Asymptomatic - Most Common):**
- **No symptoms**

**Moderate-Heavy Infection:**
- **Chronic dysentery** (bloody, mucoid diarrhea)
- **Abdominal pain, tenesmus**
- **Anemia** (chronic blood loss)
- **Growth retardation** (children)

**Severe Infection (Trichuris Dysentery Syndrome):**
- **Chronic diarrhea** (10-20 stools/day)
- **Rectal prolapse** (pathognomonic - from straining)
- **Finger clubbing**
- **Severe anemia**

**Key Point:** **Rectal prolapse = pathognomonic** for heavy whipworm infection (children)

---

### **Diagnosis:**

| **Test** | **Specimen** | **Sensitivity** | **Notes** |
|----------|--------------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Stool O&P (microscopy)** | Stool | **>90%** | **Barrel-shaped eggs** with bipolar plugs |
| **Colonoscopy** | Rectosigmoid | **100%** | **Worms visible** (anterior end embedded in mucosa) |

**Key Point:** **Barrel-shaped eggs with bipolar plugs** = Trichuris

---

### **Treatment:**

**First-Line:**

| **Agent** | **Dosing** | **Duration** | **Efficacy** |
|-----------|------------|--------------|--------------|
| **Mebendazole** (preferred) | **100 mg PO BID** OR **500 mg PO x 1 dose x 2 days** | **3 days** OR **2 consecutive days** | **More effective** than albendazole (87% egg reduction) |
| **Albendazole** | **400 mg PO x 1 dose x 3 days** | **3 days** | Lower efficacy (60% egg reduction) |

**Combination Therapy (Heavy Infection):**

| **Combination** | **Dosing** |
|-----------------|------------|
| **Albendazole + Ivermectin** | **Albendazole** 400 mg PO x 1 + **Ivermectin** 200 μg/kg PO x 1 | **Superior efficacy** (31% cure rate vs 12% albendazole alone) |

**Key Point:** **Mebendazole = more effective** than albendazole for Trichuris (cure rates poor ~50% with single-dose)

---

### **Prevention:**

- **Improved sanitation**
- **Mass drug administration** (MDA)

---

## 3. ENTEROBIUS VERMICULARIS (PINWORM / ENTEROBIASIS)

**Organism:** *Enterobius vermicularis* ("pinworm" - small, white, thread-like worms)

**Transmission:**
- **Fecal-oral** (ingestion of eggs)
- **Autoinfection** (scratching perianal area → transfer eggs to mouth)
- **Fomites** (clothing, bedding)

**Epidemiology:**
- **Most common helminth infection** in US/developed countries
- **~200 million infected** worldwide
- **Children** (daycare, schools)

---

### **Life Cycle:**

1. **Eggs ingested** → Hatch in small intestine → **Larvae**
2. **Adults** in cecum/appendix
3. **Gravid female worms migrate** to perianal area at night → **Lay eggs** (10,000-15,000)
4. **Eggs become infectious** within hours → **Autoinfection** (scratching → ingestion)

**Key Point:** **Female worms lay eggs at perianal area at night** (cause itching)

---

### **Clinical Features:**

**Perianal Pruritus (Pathognomonic):**
- **Intense itching** around anus (nocturnal - worse at night)
- **Insomnia, irritability** (children)
- **Excoriation** from scratching

**Other Manifestations:**
- **Vulvovaginitis** (female migration to vagina)
- **Appendicitis** (rare - worms in appendix)
- **Eosinophilia** (mild or absent)

**Key Point:** **Nocturnal perianal itching** = pinworm

---

### **Diagnosis:**

**"Tape Test" (Cellophane Tape Test) - GOLD STANDARD:**

**Method:**
- **Clear cellophane tape** pressed against perianal skin in morning (before bathing/defecation)
- **Transfer tape** to glass slide → Examine under microscope for eggs

**Timing:**
- **Early morning** (before bathing) - eggs laid at night
- **Repeat 3 consecutive days** (single test 50% sensitive, 3 tests 90% sensitive)

**Stool O&P:**
- **Low yield** (eggs not reliably found in stool)

**Key Point:** **Tape test = gold standard** (3 consecutive mornings → 90% sensitivity)

---

### **Treatment:**

**First-Line (Treat All Household Members):**

| **Agent** | **Dosing** | **Repeat** |
|-----------|------------|------------|
| **Albendazole** | **400 mg PO x 1 dose** | **Repeat in 2 weeks** |
| **OR Mebendazole** | **100 mg PO x 1 dose** | **Repeat in 2 weeks** |
| **OR Pyrantel pamoate** (OTC) | **11 mg/kg PO x 1 dose** (max 1 g) | **Repeat in 2 weeks** |

**Important:**
- **Treat all household members** simultaneously (prevent re-infection)
- **Repeat dose in 2 weeks** (kill newly hatched worms from eggs present after first dose)

**Pregnancy:**
- **Pyrantel pamoate** preferred (OTC, safer in pregnancy)

**Environmental Measures:**
- **Wash bedding, clothing** (hot water)
- **Vacuum carpets**
- **Trim fingernails** (reduce egg accumulation)

**Key Point:** **Treat all household members** + **repeat in 2 weeks**

---

## COMPARISON TABLE

| **Feature** | **Ascaris** | **Trichuris (Whipworm)** | **Pinworm** |
|-------------|-------------|--------------------------|-------------|
| **Size** | **Largest** (15-35 cm) | 3-5 cm | **Smallest** (0.5-1 cm) |
| **Location** | Small intestine (jejunum) | Large intestine (cecum, colon) | Cecum, appendix |
| **Key clinical feature** | **Intestinal obstruction**, Löffler syndrome | **Rectal prolapse** (heavy infection) | **Nocturnal perianal itching** |
| **Stool finding** | Oval, thick-walled eggs | **Barrel-shaped eggs** (bipolar plugs) | None (eggs at perianal area) |
| **Diagnosis** | Stool O&P | Stool O&P | **Tape test** (perianal) |
| **Treatment** | Albendazole OR Mebendazole (single dose) | **Mebendazole 3 days** (more effective than albendazole) | Albendazole OR Mebendazole (single dose, **repeat in 2 weeks**) |

---

**Mnemonic: "Ascaris = Largest Worm (Intestinal Obstruction)"**
- **A**scaris = **L**argest intestinal nematode
- **I**ntestinal **O**bstruction (most common serious complication)
- Löffler syndrome (pulmonary phase)

**Mnemonic: "Trichuris = Whip (Rectal Prolapse)"**
- **T**richuris = **W**hipworm (anterior "whip" end burrowed)
- **R**ectal **P**rolapse (pathognomonic - heavy infection)
- **Barrel-shaped eggs** (bipolar plugs)

**Mnemonic: "Pinworm = Perianal Itching at Night (Tape Test)"**
- **P**inworm = **P**erianal itching (nocturnal)
- **T**ape test (perianal, early morning)
- **T**reat all household members + **R**epeat in 2 weeks

**Mnemonic: "Mebendazole > Albendazole for Trichuris"**
- **M**ebendazole = **M**ore effective for **T**richuris (whipworm)
- Albendazole = Ascaris, pinworm (single dose)

**Clinical Pearls:**
- **Ascaris = largest intestinal nematode** (15-35 cm)
- **Löffler syndrome** = transient pulmonary infiltrates + eosinophilia (larval migration)
- **Intestinal obstruction = most common serious complication** of Ascaris (especially children)
- **Avoid paralyzing agents** (pyrantel, ivermectin) if obstruction (use albendazole/mebendazole)
- **Biliary ascariasis:** Worms in biliary tree ("spaghetti sign" on ultrasound/ERCP)
- **Trichuris = whipworm** (anterior "whip" end burrowed into colonic mucosa)
- **Rectal prolapse = pathognomonic** for heavy Trichuris infection (children)
- **Barrel-shaped eggs with bipolar plugs** = Trichuris
- **Mebendazole > albendazole** for Trichuris (87% vs 60% egg reduction)
- **Combination therapy** (albendazole + ivermectin) superior for heavy Trichuris
- **Pinworm = most common helminth in US** (children, daycare)
- **Nocturnal perianal itching** = pathognomonic for pinworm
- **Tape test = gold standard** (3 consecutive mornings → 90% sensitivity)
- **Treat all household members simultaneously** + repeat in 2 weeks
- **Pyrantel pamoate** = OTC, safe in pregnancy
