## Question

What are the key clinical syndromes and treatment of schistosomiasis? Use the **"Freshwater Exposure (Africa/Asia), Katayama Fever, Terminal Hematuria (*S. haematobium*) → Praziquantel"** framework.

## Answer

### **Schistosoma Species Overview:**

**Geography:**
- ***S. haematobium*:** Sub-Saharan Africa, Middle East (urinary)
- ***S. mansoni*:** Sub-Saharan Africa, Middle East, Caribbean, South America (intestinal)
- ***S. japonicum*:** China, Southeast Asia, Philippines (intestinal, most severe)
- ***S. mekongi, S. intercalatum*:** Southeast Asia, Central Africa (rare)

**Transmission:** Freshwater exposure (swimming, bathing) → cercariae (larvae) penetrate skin → migrate to liver → mature → adult worms in mesenteric or bladder venules

### **Clinical Syndromes:**

**Acute Schistosomiasis (Katayama Fever) - 2-8 Weeks Post-Exposure:**

**Presentation:**
- **Fever, chills, headache, myalgia** (serum sickness-like)
- **Cough, dyspnea** (pulmonary infiltrates)
- **Hepatosplenomegaly, lymphadenopathy**
- **Diarrhea** (bloody in some)
- **Urticaria, angioedema**
- **Eosinophilia** (often >50%)

**More common with:** *S. japonicum*, *S. mansoni* (primary infection in non-endemic travelers)

**Chronic Schistosomiasis:**

**Intestinal (***S. mansoni*, *S. japonicum***):**
- **Hepatosplenic disease:** Periportal fibrosis (pipestem fibrosis) → portal hypertension → varices, ascites, splenomegaly
- **GI symptoms:** Abdominal pain, diarrhea (bloody), colonic polyps ("bilharzial pseudopolyposis")

**Urogenital (***S. haematobium***):**
- **Hematuria** (terminal = end of urination) - hallmark
- **Dysuria, frequency**
- **Bladder fibrosis, calcification** (chronic inflammation)
- **Squamous cell carcinoma of bladder** (long-standing infection, 10-20× risk)
- **Female genital schistosomiasis:** Vaginal bleeding, infertility, increased HIV risk

**Neurologic:**
- **Neuroschistosomiasis (***S. mansoni*, *S. haematobium***):** Transverse myelitis, cauda equina syndrome (ectopic egg deposition in spinal cord)
- **Cerebral (***S. japonicum***):** Seizures, mass lesions (ectopic eggs in brain)

### **Diagnosis:**

**Microscopy (Gold Standard):**
- **Stool:** Ova with lateral spine (*S. mansoni*), lateral rounded spine (*S. japonicum*)
- **Urine:** Ova with terminal spine (*S. haematobium*)
- **Collect midday urine** (higher egg excretion)
- **Kato-Katz technique** (quantitative)

**Serology:**
- **Detects antibodies** (IgG)
- **Useful in travelers** (acute infection before egg production, or light infections)
- **Cannot distinguish active from past infection** (remains positive for years)

**Antigen Detection:**
- **Circulating cathodic antigen (CCA), circulating anodic antigen (CAA)**
- **Urine CCA** (point-of-care test for *S. mansoni*)

**Eosinophilia:**
- **Common** (especially acute infection)

**Imaging:**
- **Ultrasound:** Periportal fibrosis (liver), bladder wall thickening/calcification
- **CT/MRI:** CNS lesions (neuroschistosomiasis)

### **Treatment:**

**Praziquantel (Drug of Choice for ALL Species):**

**Intestinal (***S. mansoni*, *S. japonicum*, *S. mekongi*):**
- **Praziquantel 40 mg/kg/day PO divided BID × 1 day** (single-day treatment)

***S. haematobium*, *S. intercalatum*:**
- **Praziquantel 40 mg/kg PO × 1** (single dose)

***S. japonicum* (Alternative Dosing):**
- **Praziquantel 60 mg/kg/day PO divided TID × 1 day** (higher dose for *S. japonicum*)

**Acute Schistosomiasis (Katayama Fever):**
- **Corticosteroids:** Prednisone 20-40mg PO daily × 5 days (reduce immune complex symptoms)
- **THEN praziquantel 40 mg/kg BID × 1 day** (after steroids, as praziquantel less effective against immature worms, but give anyway)
- **Repeat praziquantel 4-6 weeks later** (to kill worms that matured after initial treatment)

**Neuroschistosomiasis:**
- **Corticosteroids:** Dexamethasone or prednisone (reduce inflammation from dying parasites)
- **Praziquantel 60 mg/kg/day divided TID × 3 days**
- **Longer steroid taper** (weeks)

**Treatment Failure / Drug Resistance (Rare):**
- **Repeat praziquantel** (higher dose or longer duration)
- **Artemether** (artemisinins have activity against schistosomes)

## Key Points

### **Katayama Fever:**
- **Acute serum sickness-like illness** (2-8 weeks post-exposure)
- **High eosinophilia** (>50%)
- **Treat with steroids THEN praziquantel** (repeat in 4-6 weeks)

### **Terminal Hematuria = *S. haematobium*:**
- **Hematuria at end of urination** (bladder, not kidney)
- **Bladder calcification** on imaging (chronic)
- **Squamous cell carcinoma risk** (long-standing)

### **Periportal Fibrosis = *S. mansoni/japonicum*:**
- **"Pipestem fibrosis"** (periportal, NOT cirrhosis)
- **Portal hypertension** → varices, splenomegaly
- **Liver synthetic function preserved** (not cirrhosis)

### **Praziquantel = Treatment for All:**
- **Single-day treatment** (40 mg/kg divided BID)
- **Safe, well-tolerated**
- **Take with food** (increases absorption)

### **Neuroschistosomiasis = Medical Emergency:**
- **Transverse myelitis, cauda equina** (*S. mansoni*, *S. haematobium*)
- **Seizures** (*S. japonicum*)
- **Steroids + praziquantel** (steroids first to reduce inflammation)

### **Swimmer's Itch:**
- **Cercarial dermatitis** (immediate after water exposure)
- **Pruritic papular rash** (hours after exposure)
- **Self-limited** (allergic reaction to cercariae penetration)
- **Can occur with avian/mammalian schistosomes** (NOT human species, no chronic infection)

### **Clinical Pearls:**
- **Freshwater exposure** (Africa, Asia, South America) + eosinophilia → suspect schistosomiasis
- **Katayama fever:** Acute febrile illness + eosinophilia (2-8 weeks post-exposure)
- **Terminal hematuria** = *S. haematobium* (bladder)
- **Periportal fibrosis** = *S. mansoni/japonicum* (portal hypertension, varices)
- **Praziquantel 40 mg/kg PO divided BID × 1 day** (all species)
- **Neuroschistosomiasis:** Steroids + praziquantel (transverse myelitis, seizures)
