## Clostridium botulinum (Botulism) & Clostridium tetani (Tetanus)

**Q:** What are the clinical features, pathophysiology, and treatment (including antitoxin therapy) for botulism and tetanus?

**A:**

## BOTULISM (Clostridium botulinum)

**Microbiology:**
- **Gram-positive, spore-forming anaerobic bacillus**
- Produces **botulinum neurotoxin** (most potent toxin known)
- 7 serotypes: A, B, C, D, E, F, G (human disease: A, B, E most common)

**Pathophysiology:**
- **Blocks acetylcholine release** at neuromuscular junction → flaccid paralysis
- **Descending paralysis** (cranial nerves first)

---

**Clinical Forms:**

**1. Foodborne Botulism (Most Common):**

**Source:** Improperly canned/preserved foods (home-canned vegetables, fermented fish, honey)

**Incubation:** 12-36 hours (range 6h-10 days)

**Classic Triad:**
- **Descending flaccid paralysis**
- **Absent fever**
- **Clear sensorium**

**Cranial Nerve Involvement (4 D's):**
- **D**iplopia (CN III, IV, VI)
- **D**ysarthria (CN XII)
- **D**ysphonia (CN X)
- **D**ysphagia (CN IX, X)

**Autonomic Symptoms:**
- Dry mouth, dilated pupils, constipation, urinary retention

**Progression:**
- Descending: Face → arms → respiratory muscles → legs
- **Respiratory failure** (most common cause of death)

**2. Wound Botulism:**
- Injection drug users (black tar heroin)
- Traumatic wounds contaminated with soil
- Similar clinical picture but NO GI symptoms

**3. Infant Botulism:**
- Ingestion of spores (honey) → intestinal colonization → toxin production
- "Floppy baby syndrome"
- Constipation, weak cry, poor feeding, hypotonia

**4. Iatrogenic Botulism:**
- Cosmetic/therapeutic botox overdose

---

**Diagnosis:**

**Clinical Diagnosis:**
- Descending flaccid paralysis + afebrile + clear sensorium

**Laboratory:**
- **Mouse bioassay:** Detects toxin in serum, stool, food (takes 1-2 days)
- **PCR:** Detects toxin genes
- **Culture:** C. botulinum from stool/wound

**EMG:**
- **Incremental response** to repetitive nerve stimulation (RNS) at high frequency

---

**Treatment:**

**1. Antitoxin (CRITICAL - Give Early):**

**Heptavalent Botulism Antitoxin (HBAT):**
- **Covers serotypes A, B, C, D, E, F, G**
- **Equine-derived** (Fab and F(ab')2 fragments)
- **FDA-approved 2013**, replaced all prior formulations (2010)

**Dosing:**
- **1 vial IV x1** (single dose sufficient)
- Available from **CDC** (24/7 hotline: 770-488-7100)

**Timing:**
- **Give within 24 hours** of symptom onset (ideally)
- **Do NOT wait for lab confirmation** (clinical diagnosis sufficient)
- Stops progression but does NOT reverse paralysis

**Mechanism:**
- Neutralizes **circulating toxin** (passive immunization)
- Does NOT affect toxin already bound to neurons

**Infant Botulism:**
- **BabyBIG** (Botulism Immune Globulin Intravenous - human-derived)
- Dosing: 1 mL/kg (50 mg/kg) IV x1

**2. Supportive Care:**
- **Mechanical ventilation** (30-50% require intubation)
- ICU monitoring
- NG feeding (dysphagia)

**3. Antibiotics (Wound Botulism Only):**
- **Penicillin G** 3 million units IV q4h OR **metronidazole** 500 mg IV q6h
- Debride wound
- **NOT for foodborne or infant botulism** (may ↑ toxin release from lysed bacteria)

**4. Do NOT Use:**
- Aminoglycosides (worsen neuromuscular blockade)

---

**Prognosis:**
- **Mortality:** <5% (with modern ICU care)
- **Recovery:** Weeks to months (new axonal sprouting needed)
- Full recovery in most cases

---

## TETANUS (Clostridium tetani)

**Microbiology:**
- **Gram-positive, spore-forming anaerobic bacillus**
- Produces **tetanospasmin** (neurotoxin)
- Found in soil, feces

**Pathophysiology:**
- **Blocks inhibitory neurotransmitters** (GABA, glycine) → uninhibited motor neuron activity
- **Result:** Spastic paralysis (vs botulism = flaccid)

---

**Clinical Presentation:**

**Incubation:** 3-21 days (average 8 days)

**Classic Features:**

**1. Trismus ("Lockjaw"):**
- Masseter muscle spasm
- Difficulty opening mouth

**2. Risus Sardonicus:**
- Fixed "sardonic grin" (facial muscle spasm)

**3. Opisthotonus:**
- Severe back arching (extensor muscle spasm)

**4. Generalized Spasms:**
- **Painful** muscle contractions (entire body)
- Triggered by stimuli (light, sound, touch)
- **Respiratory failure** (laryngeal/respiratory muscle spasm)

**5. Autonomic Instability:**
- Hypertension, tachycardia, arrhythmias, hyperthermia

**Key Distinguishing Feature:**
- **Ascending spastic paralysis** (starts jaw → face → trunk)
- **Muscle rigidity + painful spasms**

---

**Clinical Forms:**

**1. Generalized Tetanus (Most Common - 80%):**
- Trismus → risus sardonicus → opisthotonus → generalized spasms

**2. Localized Tetanus:**
- Muscle spasm near wound site
- May progress to generalized

**3. Cephalic Tetanus:**
- Cranial nerve dysfunction
- Often after head/face wounds

**4. Neonatal Tetanus:**
- Umbilical stump infection (contaminated cutting instruments)
- High mortality (>50%)

---

**Diagnosis:**

**Clinical Diagnosis:**
- Trismus + muscle spasms + clear sensorium + history of wound

**Spatula Test:**
- Touch posterior pharynx with tongue blade
- **Positive:** Reflex spasm of masseter (bite down) - tetanus
- **Negative:** Gag reflex (normal)
- Sensitivity ~94%, specificity 100%

**Laboratory:**
- Culture rarely helpful (C. tetani often not recovered)
- **No serologic test**

---

**Treatment:**

**1. Human Tetanus Immune Globulin (HTIG):**

**Dosing:**
- **500 units IM x1** (some recommend 3,000-6,000 units)
- **Give as soon as possible**

**Mechanism:**
- Neutralizes **circulating toxin**
- Does NOT affect toxin already in CNS

**Alternative (if HTIG unavailable):**
- **IVIG** 400 mg/kg IV x1 (contains anti-tetanus antibodies)

**2. Wound Management:**
- **Debride wound** (remove necrotic tissue, foreign material)
- Reduce anaerobic environment

**3. Antibiotics:**

**First-Line:**
- **Metronidazole** 500 mg IV q6h x 7-10 days (preferred)
- **Alternative:** Penicillin G 2-4 million units IV q4-6h

**Rationale:**
- Kill vegetative C. tetani
- Prevent toxin production

**4. Muscle Spasm Control:**

**Benzodiazepines (First-Line):**
- **Diazepam** 5-10 mg IV q3-6h (escalate as needed, up to 100-300 mg/day)
- **Lorazepam** 1-2 mg IV q4-6h

**If Refractory:**
- **Baclofen** (GABA agonist)
- **Magnesium sulfate** (reduces muscle spasm, autonomic instability)
- **Neuromuscular blockade + mechanical ventilation** (severe cases)

**5. Supportive Care:**
- **ICU monitoring**
- **Mechanical ventilation** (if respiratory compromise)
- **Minimize stimuli** (quiet, dark room)
- **Tracheostomy** (if prolonged intubation needed)
- **Autonomic instability management:**
  - β-blockers (esmolol, labetalol)
  - Magnesium sulfate

**6. Vaccination (After Recovery):**
- **Tetanus does NOT confer immunity** (toxin amount too low)
- **Give tetanus toxoid vaccine series** after recovery

---

**Prognosis:**

**Mortality:**
- **Generalized tetanus:** 10-20% (with modern ICU care)
- **Neonatal tetanus:** >50%
- **Cephalic tetanus:** Very high mortality

**Predictors of Poor Outcome:**
- **Short incubation** (<7 days)
- **Short period of onset** (time from first symptom to first spasm <48h)
- **Extremes of age** (neonates, elderly)
- **Inadequate wound care**

**Recovery:**
- Weeks to months
- Spasms gradually improve

---

**Prevention:**

**Tetanus Prophylaxis (Wound Management):**

| **Wound Type** | **Vaccination History** | **Tdap/Td** | **HTIG** |
|----------------|-------------------------|-------------|----------|
| **Clean, minor** | ≥3 doses, last <10 years ago | No | No |
| **Clean, minor** | <3 doses OR unknown | Yes | No |
| **Tetanus-prone** | ≥3 doses, last <5 years ago | No | No |
| **Tetanus-prone** | ≥3 doses, last 5-10 years ago | Yes | No |
| **Tetanus-prone** | <3 doses OR unknown | Yes | **Yes (250 units IM)** |

**Tetanus-Prone Wounds:**
- Contaminated with dirt, feces, saliva
- Puncture wounds
- Crush injuries
- Burns, frostbite
- Necrotic tissue

---

**Comparison: Botulism vs Tetanus**

| **Feature** | **Botulism** | **Tetanus** |
|-------------|--------------|-------------|
| **Organism** | C. botulinum | C. tetani |
| **Toxin** | Botulinum toxin | Tetanospasmin |
| **Mechanism** | Blocks ACh release | Blocks inhibitory neurotransmitters |
| **Paralysis** | **Flaccid** (descending) | **Spastic** (ascending) |
| **Trismus** | ❌ No | ✅ Yes (lockjaw) |
| **Cranial nerves** | ✅ Yes (4 D's) | ✅ Yes (facial spasm) |
| **Fever** | ❌ Afebrile | ❌ Afebrile (unless autonomic instability) |
| **Muscle spasm** | ❌ Flaccid weakness | ✅ **Painful spasms** |
| **Antitoxin** | **HBAT** (equine, heptavalent) | **HTIG** (human) |
| **Recovery** | Weeks-months (new axons) | Weeks-months (toxin clearance) |

---

**Mnemonic: "Botulism = Blocks ACh, Flaccid, Descending"**
- **B**locks acetylcholine
- **F**laccid paralysis
- **D**escending (cranial nerves first)

**Mnemonic: "Tetanus = Tense muscles, Ascending"**
- **T**ense (spastic, rigid)
- **A**scending (jaw first → face → body)

**Clinical Pearls:**
- **Botulism: "Can't see, can't speak, can't swallow"** (4 D's: diplopia, dysarthria, dysphonia, dysphagia)
- **Tetanus: "Can't open mouth, can't relax"** (trismus, opisthotonus)
- **HBAT for botulism within 24h** (stops progression, CDC hotline 770-488-7100)
- **HTIG for tetanus 500 units IM** (neutralizes circulating toxin)
- **Botulism = flaccid, tetanus = spastic**
- **Both afebrile with clear sensorium**
- **Spatula test** for tetanus (94% sensitivity, 100% specificity)
- **Tetanus does NOT confer immunity** (vaccinate after recovery)
- **Avoid aminoglycosides in botulism** (worsen neuromuscular blockade)
- **EMG in botulism:** Incremental response at high-frequency RNS
- **Wound botulism in IVDU** (black tar heroin)
- **Infant botulism from honey** (floppy baby)
