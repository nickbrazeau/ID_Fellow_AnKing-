## Poststreptococcal Sequelae: Rheumatic Fever and Glomerulonephritis

**Q:** What are the Jones criteria for acute rheumatic fever and how does PSGN differ?

**A:**

**Acute Rheumatic Fever (ARF):**

**Revised Jones Criteria (Diagnosis):**

**Requires:** Evidence of recent GAS infection (elevated ASO, anti-DNase B, positive throat culture, or rapid antigen test)

**PLUS:** 2 major OR 1 major + 2 minor criteria

**Major Criteria (J❤️NCES):**
- **J**oints: Migratory polyarthritis
- **❤️ (Carditis)**: Valvulitis (mitral regurg most common); myocarditis; pericarditis
- **N**odules: Subcutaneous nodules (painless, extensor surfaces)
- **C**horea: Sydenham chorea ("St. Vitus dance" - involuntary movements)
- **E**rythema marginatum: Erythematous, serpiginous rash (trunk/proximal extremities)

**Minor Criteria:**
- Fever
- Polyarthralgias
- **Elevated ESR or CRP**
- **Prolonged PR interval** on ECG

**Key Features:**
- Peak age: **5-15 years**
- **Latency**: 2-3 weeks post-pharyngitis (NOT after impetigo)
- **Carditis**: Can lead to chronic RHD (mitral stenosis years later)
- Treatment: Penicillin (eradicate GAS), NSAIDs/aspirin (arthritis), corticosteroids (severe carditis)
- **Secondary prophylaxis**: Long-term penicillin to prevent recurrence

---

**Poststreptococcal Glomerulonephritis (PSGN):**

**Clinical Features:**
- **Latency**: 1-3 weeks post-pharyngitis, **1-6 weeks post-impetigo**
- **Age**: Children 5-12 years (can occur in adults)
- **Triad**: Hematuria (tea-colored urine) + proteinuria + edema
- **Hypertension**, oliguria
- **Nephritic syndrome**: Active urinary sediment (RBCs, RBC casts, WBCs)

**Laboratory:**
- **Low C3** (returns to normal in 6-8 weeks)
- Elevated ASO/anti-DNase B
- Hematuria, proteinuria (usually <3.5 g/day)

**Prognosis:**
- Children: **>95% full recovery**
- Adults: Higher risk of chronic kidney disease

**Treatment:**
- **Supportive**: Fluid/salt restriction, antihypertensives, diuretics
- **Antibiotics** (if active GAS infection; does NOT alter PSGN course)

---

**ARF vs PSGN Comparison:**

| Feature | ARF | PSGN |
|---------|-----|------|
| **Latency** | 2-3 weeks | 1-3 weeks (pharyngitis), 1-6 weeks (impetigo) |
| **Follows impetigo?** | **NO** | **YES** |
| **Complement** | Normal | **Low C3** |
| **Prognosis** | **Chronic RHD risk** | Children: excellent (>95% recover); Adults: CKD risk |
| **Concurrent occurrence** | **Rare** (different GAS strains: rheumatogenic vs nephritogenic) |

**Key Points:**
- **ARF**: J❤️NCES criteria; 2-3 weeks post-pharyngitis; risk of chronic RHD (mitral stenosis)
- **PSGN**: Low C3, nephritic syndrome; follows pharyngitis OR impetigo; excellent prognosis in children
- Both are **immune-mediated** (NON-suppurative sequelae)
- **Rare to occur together** (different GAS strains)

**Mnemonic - "ARF J❤️NCES Hearts, PSGN C3 Kidneys"**: ARF affects heart (Jones criteria), PSGN lowers C3 and affects kidneys

**Pearl:** ARF requires 2 major or 1 major + 2 minor Jones criteria (J❤️NCES: Joints, Carditis, Nodules, Chorea, Erythema marginatum) plus GAS evidence. PSGN has low C3 and excellent prognosis in children (>95% recover). Both follow pharyngitis, but ONLY PSGN follows impetigo.
